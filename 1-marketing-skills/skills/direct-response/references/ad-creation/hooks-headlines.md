## The 3 Questions Before Writing Any Headline

From Breakthrough Advertising by Eugene Schwartz, adapted by Jeremy:

1. **What is the mass desire that creates the market?**
   - Mass desire = an existing want inside the market you're marketing to
   - You're tapping into it, not creating a new desire
   - If a headline doesn't have mass desire integrated, people won't respond

2. **How much do they know about how your product satisfies this desire? (State of Awareness)**
   - Do they know your brand exists?
   - Do they know solutions exist for their problem?
   - Are there competitors already marketing to them?
   - This dictates the verbiage in your headline

3. **How many other products have been presented to them before yours? (State of Sophistication)**
   - Stage 1: Direct claim -- "Hey, we've got this. Come buy it."
   - Stage 2: Same type of headline, just outbid/outcompete everyone
   - Stage 3: Communicate the HOW -- how you produce a result
   - Stage 4 (hardest): Talk about the MECHANISM -- the process to getting the result

## 3 Steps in Developing a Headline

1. Name the desire or its solution in the headline
2. Prove that the solution can be accomplished
3. Show that the mechanism of that accomplishment is contained in your product

Write a few headlines, then checklist each against these three criteria.

## 5 Rules for Writing Great Headlines

1. **Self-interest first** -- Every headline must answer "what's in it for me?" for the reader. Not YOUR self-interest -- the MARKET'S self-interest. Incorporate mass desire.

2. **If you have news, lead with it** -- New product, new use for old product, featured placement. Study how Fox/CNN write headlines that provoke interest. Best for markets that haven't heard of you yet.

3. **Avoid headlines that merely provoke curiosity** -- Curiosity combined with news or self-interest is excellent. Curiosity alone is seldom enough. A well-crafted headline can inspire action by itself.

4. **Avoid gloomy/negative headlines** -- Take the cheerful and positive angle. People want HOPE. Use the downside in body copy, but headlines should focus on the upside.

5. **Suggest a quick and easy way** -- Imply the solution isn't complex. But don't over-imply and make it sound spammy. Avoid noncompliant claims (comparing business to "putting something in a microwave" = implied claim).

## 38 Ways to Strengthen a Headline (from Breakthrough Advertising)

1. Measure the SIZE of the claim
2. Measure the SPEED of the claim
3. Compare the claim to its unnamed rival
4. Metaphorize the claim
5. Sensitize the claim -- make the prospect feel, smell, touch, see, or hear it (sells to kinesthetic 40%+ of population -- VAK system)
6. Demonstrate the claim with a prime example
7. Dramatize the claim or its result ("They laughed when I sat at the piano, but when I started to play...")
8. State the claim as a paradox
9. Remove limitations from the claim
10. Associate the claim with values/people the prospect wants to be identified with (affinity trigger)
11. Show how much work/detail the claim does (3rd stage market sophistication)
12. State the claim as a question ("Who else wants whiter teeth with no hard work?")
13. Offer information about how to accomplish the claim
14. Tie authority into the claim before and after
15. Stress the NEWNESS of the claim
16. Stress the EXCLUSIVITY of the claim
17. Turn the claim into a challenge for the reader
18. State the claim as a case history quotation
19. Condense the claim
20. Interchange your product and the product it replaces
21. Symbolize the claim -- replace direct statement with parallel reality
22. Connect the mechanism of the claim in the headline (4th stage sophistication)
23. Startle the reader by contradicting how they think the mechanism should work (great for high-skepticism)
24. Connect the need and the claim in the headline
25. Offer information in the ad itself
26. Turn the claim of the need into a case history
27. Give a name to the problem or need
28. Warn the reader about pitfalls if they don't use the product
29. Emphasize by paraphraseology -- break into two sentences or repeat part of it
30. Show how easy the claim is by imposing universally overcome limitations
31. State the difference in the headline
32. Surprise reader into realizing former limitations have been overcome
33. Address people who CAN'T buy your product (qualify)
34. Address your prospect directly ("To the man who will settle for nothing less than the presidency of his firm")
35. Dramatize how hard it would be to produce the claim
36. Accuse the claim of being too good ("Is it immoral to make money this easily?")
37. Challenge the prospect's present limiting beliefs ("You're twice as smart as you think")
38. Turn the claim into a question and answer

## Headline Writing Rules

1. **Self-Interest First** -- Every headline answers "what's in it for me?"
2. **Specificity Wins** -- "Make $10,000/month" beats "Make more money." Numbers, timeframes, specifics always outperform vague claims
3. **Target The Right Audience** -- Call out who you're talking to. If talking to dentists, say "dentists" in the headline
4. **Combine Elements** -- Best headlines combine 2-3 categories. Example: Question + Number + Identity = "Are You Making These 5 Mistakes That Cost Real Estate Agents Thousands?"
5. **Test Everything** -- Headlines are the single most impactful element. Test 5-10 variations minimum
6. **First Line = First Impression** -- If the headline doesn't stop the scroll, nothing else matters
7. **Avoid Clickbait Without Substance** -- Curiosity is powerful only when combined with self-interest or news
8. **Match Awareness Level** -- Unaware audiences need education-first. Aware audiences want specific claims and offers
