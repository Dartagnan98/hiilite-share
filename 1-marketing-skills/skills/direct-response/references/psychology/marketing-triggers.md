# The 9 Marketing Triggers & Cognitive Biases

Source: Jeremy Haynes / Master Internet Marketing, Week 1 Lessons 16-25
Framework: Cognitive biases and psychological triggers that get people to take specific actions. These come after understanding the 13 Emotional Manipulators and build on the Breakthrough Advertising principles.

---

## Foundation: What Makes a Human Take Action

Before the triggers, understand the three dimensions of thought and feeling that drive ALL buying behavior:

### 1. Desires
Wants, needs, cravings, thirsts that drive behavior. What they want to have, achieve, or become.

### 2. Identifications
Roles or characteristics your prospect wants to play in life. The traits they want to build. Your task is to put these traits directly behind your product: "By buying this, I am ___."
- Example: Jeremy spent $15,500 on recovery devices because he identifies as "peak performance"
- Any product aligned to that identity "almost immediately has a piece of my wallet"
- Make prospects feel part of a select group when they become a user of your product

### 3. Beliefs
Opinions, prejudices, fragments of knowledge, and conceptions of reality they live by. Beliefs dictate behavior almost as much as identifications.
- **You cannot easily change beliefs.** It is expensive and slow.
- Instead, align your product to a group that already holds specific beliefs
- Beliefs form a filter through which your product information must pass or be rejected
- Example: Environmentalists already believe in reducing carbon footprint. Sell them products that align to that belief rather than trying to convince non-environmentalists
- **Believing is a process of fitting new facts into established patterns of thought**

### Application
Start with these beliefs as a base and build up using your prospect's logic to prove:
1. Your product satisfies their desires
2. Your product works
3. Their kind of people rely on your product

## Trigger 1: Honesty

**Definition:** Be truthful in everything you say, almost to the point where you are disarmingly truthful. Radical honesty breaks down skepticism and creates trust.

**Case Study: Burn the Ships (Judge Graham & Matt Monero)**
- Two 9-figure business owners creating a mentoring program
- Targeting business owners who had already heard every claim from everyone
- Stage 4 market sophistication -- need to talk about process, not just "what" they offer
- Solution: Used radical honesty in opt-in videos, ad videos, trust funnels, and application funnels

**The Script That Worked:**
"Hey guys. To be clear, we want to bribe you into coming to our boot camp through giving you some of the literal best information we can to show you we know what we're talking about. We're real. We're not fake gurus. We're nine-figure business owners who've been there, done that. This four-part video series is our bribe to you, completely free. We're trying to get you to come to our boot camp. We want you to fill out an application as soon as you're sold."

**Why It Worked:**
- Broke down skepticism by stating the obvious sales intent upfront
- Created trust through transparency
- Lowered the guard of business owners making $1M+ who had been pitched thousands of times
- Peppered in visuals while communicating to reinforce honesty visually

**How to Deploy:**
- Ask: "What truth are we NOT communicating right now that could increase trust and lower skepticism?"
- State your intent openly (yes, we are selling -- here is why it is worth it)
- Do not fabricate honesty -- be genuinely transparent
- Works best in stage 3-4 market sophistication where audiences are jaded

## Trigger 2: Mental Engagement

**Definition:** Challenge the mental process of the reader/viewer without making it too obvious. When done right, the prospect maintains a focused sense of engagement that leaves them with good feelings toward your message.

**Why Social Media Gets 53% of Internet Attention:**
Variable rewards keep people mentally engaged -- challenging specific beliefs, inspiring, educating. Every few posts does something that makes them feel like they are expanding what they know.

**The Retention Problem:**
- Average retention rate: 10-30% of what you are exposed to
- 10% retention = need 10 exposures for full absorption
- 30% retention = need 3 exposures for full absorption
- Mental engagement increases retention probability

**How to Deploy:**
- Challenge existing beliefs (build on them or present a different path)
- Drop unexpected insights that make viewers think
- Do not make it too obvious -- subtle challenges beat heavy-handed ones
- Use in webinars, trust funnels, documentaries, infomercials, education-based ads
- If content does not immediately challenge or stimulate, people skip past it
- Critical for education-route products/services

## Trigger 3: Harmonize

**Definition:** Get your prospect to agree with your truthful and accurate statements and start nodding their heads in agreement. Group think is extremely powerful and gets people to take action much faster.

**The Mechanism:**
When multiple people agree publicly, the remaining holdouts feel pressure to agree. They reconsider because "if that many people think this, maybe I need to consider it too."

**Live Event Application:**
- "Raise your hand if you agree with that" -- everyone raising their hand pressures non-raisers to participate
- Getting people to agree with a statement about buying being beneficial, then leveraging that group energy

**Digital Application:**
- Legitimate reviews on e-commerce product pages
- Comment sections with aligned sentiment
- Live chat during webinars and Facebook/Instagram Live
- Social proof widgets showing real-time activity
- Testimonial walls where group agreement is visible

**How to Deploy:**
- Start with statements that are obviously true (micro-agreements)
- Build from easy agreements to bigger ones
- Use social proof elements to show "everyone agrees"
- In copy: use rhetorical questions the reader will mentally answer "yes" to
- On pages: show reviews, comments, testimonials that all point the same direction
- In webinars: use polls, chat engagement, hand-raising

## Trigger 4: Curiosity

**Definition:** Use this trigger to keep the prospect interested and involved until the very end of your presentation. Curiosity almost overrides anything else someone is currently doing.

**The Neuroscience:** Curiosity is an uncontrollable behavior wired for detecting potential danger, now rewired around buying motives. Until someone figures out what peaked their curiosity, they stay engaged.

**Real-World Examples:**
- Apple Store lines: Walking past a huge crowd outside a store immediately arouses curiosity. Even in 30-degree weather in SoHo, people who had no intention of stopping joined the line
- Jeremy going down the elevator to investigate what appeared to be rabbits outside his penthouse -- completely out of his way, driven purely by curiosity

**Key Insight:** Curiosity overrides current behavior. If you create a curiosity gap, people will abandon their current path to close it.

**How to Deploy:**
- Create information gaps -- reveal enough to interest, withhold enough to compel
- Use open loops in video ads (tease something coming later)
- Headlines: "The 3-word phrase that changed everything..." (specific but vague)
- IMPORTANT: Curiosity alone is seldom enough. Combine with news or self-interest for maximum pulling power
- Do not just be mysterious -- be mysteriously valuable

**Ad Copy Patterns:**
- "What I discovered after spending $X on..."
- "The method nobody talks about for..."
- "Why everything you know about X is backwards..."
- "The hidden pattern only [percentage] notice..."

## Trigger 5: Hope

**Definition:** An implied hope tied to your product can be a very strong motivational factor in causing your prospect to buy. One of the most powerful driving forces of all the marketing triggers.

**Core Principle:** Most business is just problems and solutions. Your product IS the hope -- the light at the end of the tunnel, the escape route, the path back to a positive situation.

**Application Approach:**
- Be the positive outcome people are hopeful for
- Make your product feel within reach
- Glorify your product as the opportunity to get back to positive circumstances
- Inspire the prospect through your marketing

**Real-World Examples:**
- Rehab centers: Leveraging hope to get people through dark times. Marketing copy positions the program as the light at the end of the tunnel
- Any problem-solution business: Hope that this investment will actually solve the thing they have been struggling with

**How to Deploy:**
- In copy: paint the after-state vividly
- Show the transformation from current struggle to achieved result
- Use testimonials that demonstrate the journey from hopeless to hopeful to successful
- Position every touchpoint as a step closer to their desired outcome
- Be the positive outcome in your copy -- this is the lesson in a nutshell

## Trigger 6: Patterning

**Definition:** If you have a product to sell and others have sold a similar product successfully, find out how they did it and pattern your approach to theirs. Repeat successful actions. Do not copy -- adapt.

**Key Distinction:** Pattern, do not plagiarize. Copy the strategy and framework, not the literal words and creative.

**Spy Tools for Competitive Research:**
- **Facebook Ad Library:** See active running ads and time frames. If an ad has been running for over a year, it is likely successful.
- **SpyFu.com:** PPC history tab shows current and historical pay-per-click ads in search engines
- **YouTube:** Track competitor video ads -- click through, note view counts, posting dates. Deduce spending based on view volume over time.

**How to Deploy:**
1. Identify successful competitors in your space
2. Audit their active ads (Facebook Ad Library, SpyFu, YouTube)
3. Note which ads have been running longest (proven performers)
4. Identify the strategy, angles, and frameworks they use
5. Adapt the successful patterns to your product/offer
6. Once successful with patterns, start pioneering your own approaches

## Trigger 7: Familiarity

**Definition:** People are much more likely to buy if they are familiar with the brand name, product, or company offering the product. The more familiar a prospect is with the brand, the more inclined they are to accept and buy.

**Core Principle:** It pays to be known. Pay to be known in the marketplace of users you want buying your product in the future.

**Real-World Examples:**
- Hi-Chew vs Starburst: Jeremy hesitated to try Hi-Chew despite friends saying it was better, because he was more familiar with Starburst
- Gymshark: Spent heavily on influencer marketing, captured majority market share of activewear through Instagram. Became a 9-figure company because they were the most familiar brand

**How to Deploy:**
- Invest in brand awareness advertising (content distribution, engagement campaigns)
- Correlates directly to ad strategies like the Venus Fly Trap, Forester, and Tornado
- Run content in front of cold audiences BEFORE pitching them
- Consistency in showing up matters more than any single brilliant ad
- Familiarity bias is the core psychological principle behind every content-first ad strategy

## Trigger 8: Specificity

**Definition:** When you use specifics, your advertising copy becomes a lot more believable. By using specifics, you enhance your offer and make it more credible.

**The Psychology:** Specific numbers signal truth. Round numbers signal fabrication.

**Real-World Examples:**
- Jeremy's first webinar: "How this 22-year-old made $532,372.51" vs "made $500,000." The specific number instantly increased believability
- Scarcity: "There are only 13 spots left" is more believable than "limited spots available"

**How to Deploy:**
- Never round numbers in headlines or claims -- use exact figures
- Be specific about quantities, timeframes, and results
- For scarcity: use actual inventory numbers, not vague language
- In testimonials: "$47,283 in 60 days" beats "almost $50K in a couple months"
- People are hip to manufactured scarcity now -- be legitimately specific

## Trigger 9: Guilt (Reciprocity)

**Definition:** Give something to somebody and you automatically engender a feeling of reciprocity. NOT the same as guilt as an emotional manipulator (#5). This is the law of reciprocity applied as a marketing trigger.

**Core Mechanism:** When you give someone value first, they feel obligated to give back. This is why free content, lead magnets, and opt-ins drive sales.

**Why This Works:**
- This is why opt-ins work
- This is why webinars work
- This is why free checklists, PDFs, strategy documents, blogs, and videos drive sales
- The feeling: "This person has given me so much -- I need to buy their stuff"

**Real-World Examples:**
- Information product space: Free training creates obligation to buy the paid program
- Car trade-in: Dealer gives extra $3K on trade-in. Now with $8K in credits, buyer feels inclined to buy nicer car
- Mall food samples: Free chicken from Chinese restaurant makes you more likely to eat there vs the one that gave nothing
- Trust funnels: 4-part free video series creates massive reciprocity before the pitch

**How to Deploy:**
- Lead with value. Give your best stuff away for free.
- The more genuinely valuable the free content, the stronger the reciprocity
- Structure: Free value first -> build obligation -> make the offer
- Do not give junk and expect reciprocity -- the free thing must actually help them

## Stacking Triggers for Maximum Effect

The most effective campaigns stack multiple triggers together:

**Example Stack: Webinar Funnel**
1. **Curiosity** -- headline creates information gap
2. **Hope** -- description promises transformation
3. **Honesty** -- webinar opens with radical transparency
4. **Mental Engagement** -- content challenges existing beliefs
5. **Specificity** -- results shown with exact numbers
6. **Harmonize** -- live chat shows others agreeing
7. **Guilt/Reciprocity** -- massive free value before the pitch

**Example Stack: Application Funnel**
1. **Familiarity** -- content-first strategy (VFT/Forester) builds awareness
2. **Honesty** -- trust funnel videos are transparently direct
3. **Specificity** -- case studies use exact numbers
4. **Hope** -- testimonials show transformation
5. **Guilt/Reciprocity** -- free strategy session creates obligation
