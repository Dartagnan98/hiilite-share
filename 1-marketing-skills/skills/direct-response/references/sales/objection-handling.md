# Objection Handling Scripts

Source: MIM Bonus - Sales Training with Jeremy Haynes (Lessons 11-20)

---

## Objection Handling Philosophy

Common objections covered from multiple niches: info products, investor/capital raising, automation businesses, and agencies. These scripts come from real objections provided by Jeremy's clients and their sales teams.

---

## Objection 1: "What happens if you go out of business?"

### The Concern
Lead is worried about investing in a company that might not exist in 6 months.

### Framework
1. Acknowledge the concern as valid
2. Show proof of longevity and stability (years in business, client count, revenue)
3. Explain what protections are in place (contracts, deliverables, IP ownership)
4. Flip: "The bigger risk is staying where you are right now"
5. Social proof: reference long-term clients

---

## Objection 2: "How do I know my money is safe?"

### The Concern
Trust issue -- they don't know you well enough to hand over money.

### Framework
1. Empathize: "Totally fair question"
2. Explain your payment structure (escrow, milestone payments, guarantees)
3. Reference social proof (client count, testimonials, case studies)
4. Offer proof: "Here are clients you can talk to directly"
5. Explain your legal protections (contracts, refund policies)

---

## Objection 3: "Can I speak to the owner?"

### The Concern
Lead doesn't trust the salesperson has authority or full knowledge.

### Framework
1. Don't get defensive
2. "Absolutely, I understand wanting to speak to the top. Let me share why I can help you right now..."
3. Establish YOUR authority and expertise
4. Offer to loop in the owner if specific questions remain
5. Often this is a stall tactic -- address the real concern underneath

---

## Objection 4: "How can you guarantee I'll make money?"

### The Concern
Risk aversion -- they want certainty before investing.

### Framework
1. Be honest: "No one can guarantee results because there are variables on both sides"
2. Show what IS in your control (strategy, execution, optimization)
3. Show track record: "Here's what we've done for clients in similar situations"
4. Quantify: "Our average client sees X result in Y timeframe"
5. Flip the risk: "What's the guarantee you'll make money if you DON'T do this?"
6. Reframe: "The guarantee is our commitment to working until it works"

---

## Objection 5: "Why are you better than everybody else?"

### The Concern
Comparison shopping -- they're evaluating multiple options.

### Framework
1. Don't trash competitors
2. Focus on YOUR differentiators (process, results, team, approach)
3. Specific case studies relevant to their situation
4. "Here's what makes us different..." (2-3 concrete points)
5. Social proof: "Here's why clients chose us over alternatives"
6. Inversion: "What matters most to you in making this decision?" (then address THAT)

---

## Objection 6: "I'd rather get started next quarter"

### The Concern
Timing/procrastination -- not urgent enough to act now.

### Framework
1. "I totally understand. Can I ask -- what changes between now and next quarter?"
2. Quantify cost of waiting: "Every month you wait is $X in missed revenue"
3. "If we start now, by next quarter you'll already have [result]. If you wait, you'll be starting from zero"
4. Address the real concern (usually money or fear, not timing)
5. "Is there something specific that would need to happen for the timing to feel right?"
6. Offer a softer start: "We could begin with [smaller commitment] now"

---

## Objection 7: "I have to get my lawyer to review before moving forward"

### The Concern
Legal protection OR stall tactic.

### Framework
1. "Absolutely, I respect that. How long does your lawyer typically need?"
2. Send the contract/agreement immediately so the lawyer can review
3. Set a specific follow-up date: "Can we reconnect on [date] after the review?"
4. "Is there anything specific in the agreement that concerns you?"
5. Address specific concerns directly if possible
6. Keep momentum: "While your lawyer reviews, let's get [preliminary step] started"
7. Often a stall -- if they keep delaying, the real objection is something else

---

## Objection 8: "My business partner needs to review this"

### The Concern
Not the sole decision maker OR using partner as an excuse.

### Framework
1. "Great -- is your partner aware you're exploring this?"
2. "What would your partner need to know to feel good about this?"
3. Offer to schedule a follow-up call WITH both partners
4. Provide materials they can share: one-pager, video, case studies
5. "What's the typical process when you two evaluate opportunities like this?"
6. If possible, get the partner on the call NOW: "Can we give them a quick call?"
7. Pre-handle this in the application: "Are you the decision maker?"

---

## Objection 9: "I'm traveling this week, let's do this next week"

### The Concern
Legitimate scheduling conflict OR avoidance.

### Framework
1. "No problem -- let's lock in a specific time next week right now"
2. Book the follow-up call DURING this call
3. "In the meantime, I'll send you some materials to review"
4. Send value content to keep them warm during the gap
5. Confirm the follow-up 24 hours before
6. If they cancel the follow-up, the objection was something else
7. "I know you're busy -- is there a 15-minute window this week to at least get [next step] moving?"

## Universal Objection Handling Framework

### Step 1: Listen and Acknowledge
- Don't interrupt
- "I totally understand"
- "That's a fair concern"
- Show empathy, not defensiveness

### Step 2: Isolate the Real Objection
- "Is that the main thing holding you back?"
- "If we could resolve that, would you feel good about moving forward?"
- Often the stated objection isn't the real one

### Step 3: Address with Evidence
- Case studies, testimonials, data
- Specific examples from similar clients
- Use THEIR words from discovery

### Step 4: Confirm Resolution
- "Does that address your concern?"
- "How do you feel about that?"
- If yes, re-close. If no, dig deeper.

### Step 5: Re-Close
- "Great -- so shall we get started?"
- Don't be afraid to ask for the sale again
- Silence after asking is your friend

---

## Key Principles

1. Every objection is a buying signal -- they wouldn't object if they weren't considering it
2. Isolate the real objection before trying to handle it
3. Use evidence (case studies, data) not opinions
4. Pre-handle common objections in marketing materials and VSLs
5. The best objection handling is deep discovery (surface concerns early)
6. "I need to think about it" usually means money or fear
7. Set specific follow-up times -- "I'll call you next week" is too vague
8. Partner/spouse objection should be handled pre-call in the application
9. Never argue, get defensive, or pressure
10. Follow up 5-7 times after an unresolved objection before moving on
