## How to Think as a Copywriter

### The Expert Curse

You know too much about your industry. That's your biggest copywriting liability.

> "Because you know so much, sometimes that can make it feel like even if you reduced what you think you know down to ten percent volume, you'd still be far more advanced than everybody who you're potentially selling to." -- Jeremy Haynes

**The PayPal Example:**
- BAD: "We have a tightly integrated financial infrastructure that can process all your banking needs under a single financial institution."
- GOOD: "We can send money to your email address."

### Rule: Always Simplify
- Write at a 5th-8th grade reading level
- If your grandmother wouldn't understand it, rewrite it
- One idea per sentence, one main point per paragraph
- Your job is to communicate, not to impress

## Core Copywriting Principles

### 1. Mass Desire Comes First

From Breakthrough Advertising: mass desire is an existing idea, an existing want inside the market you're trying to reach. You tap into it. You don't create new desires -- that costs too much money.

Before writing anything, identify:
- What do they already want?
- What language do they use to describe their problem?
- What would their ideal outcome look like in their own words?

### 2. Understand the Three Dimensions

Before writing copy, you must know:

**Dimension 1: Mass Desire** -- What does the market want?
**Dimension 2: State of Awareness** -- Do they know about you, your product category, or even their problem?
**Dimension 3: State of Sophistication** -- How many competing products have they already seen?

These three dimensions dictate:
- Your headline approach
- Your body copy structure
- Your proof requirements
- Your level of directness

### 3. The VAK System (Neurolinguistic Programming)

From Richard Bandler's NLP framework. Your audience processes information in three primary modes:

- **Visual (35%)**: Respond to imagery, demonstrations, "picture this," "imagine seeing"
- **Auditory (25%)**: Respond to stories told well, "sounds like," "listen to this"
- **Kinesthetic (40%+)**: Respond to feelings, senses, emotions -- "feel the weight," "imagine the relief," "sense the freedom"

Over 40% of your audience is kinesthetic. Your copy should make them FEEL the result, not just see it.

**Application in copy:**
- "Imagine waking up and checking your phone to see $5,000 in new sales" (visual)
- "What would it sound like if your phone kept buzzing with new client notifications?" (auditory)
- "Feel the weight lift off your shoulders when you never have to worry about lead flow again" (kinesthetic)

## The Copywriter's Job

### You're Not Writing -- You're Selling in Print

Your copy must do what a salesperson does:
1. Get attention (hook/headline)
2. Build rapport and trust (story/credibility)
3. Identify and agitate the problem
4. Present the solution
5. Handle objections
6. Create urgency
7. Close (CTA)

### Headlines Are More Important Than Body Copy

> "A well-crafted headline can be more effective than your entire body copy." -- Jeremy Haynes

This is why great copywriters spend most of their time on headlines. The headline determines:
- Whether anyone reads your copy at all
- The frame through which they interpret everything that follows
- Whether they take action or scroll past

### Direct Response Copy vs. Branding Copy

**Direct Response Copy:**
- Has a specific, measurable goal (click, buy, sign up, call)
- Includes a clear CTA
- Can be tracked and optimized
- Written to convert NOW
- Speaks to the individual ("you")
- Uses urgency and specificity

**Branding Copy:**
- Builds awareness and recognition over time
- May not have a direct CTA
- Harder to measure
- Written for long-term recall
- Speaks to the collective
- Uses repetition and consistency

For direct response marketers: your copy should always be direct response FIRST. Brand second.

## 7 Techniques to Strengthen Your Copy

(From Breakthrough Advertising)

### 1. Intensification
- Take the core claim and make it more vivid, more urgent, more specific
- Add numbers, timeframes, sensory details
- Turn a mild promise into a powerful one

### 2. Identification
- Make the reader see themselves in the copy
- Use "you" language
- Reference their specific situation, fears, and desires
- Mirror their language back to them

### 3. Gradualization
- Don't ask for the big commitment immediately
- Ease them in: small agreement -> bigger agreement -> purchase
- Progressive commitment through the copy
- Each paragraph should earn the right to the next

### 4. Redefinition
- Take a known concept and redefine it in a new way
- Give your method/system a unique name
- Reframe the problem so your solution is the only logical answer
- "It's not about working harder, it's about [your unique mechanism]"

### 5. Mechanization
- Show the mechanism behind your claim
- HOW does it work? (at a high level)
- The more sophisticated the market, the more mechanism you need to show
- Give enough detail to create belief but not enough to DIY

### 6. Concentration
- Focus your copy on ONE big idea
- Don't try to say everything
- Pick the single most powerful angle and drive it home
- Supporting points should reinforce the main idea, not compete with it

### 7. Camouflage
- When a market is highly skeptical, disguise the sales pitch
- Wrap selling in education, entertainment, or story
- The reader should feel like they're learning, not being sold to
- Native content, editorial-style copy, story-driven ads

## How to Increase Believability

### Proof Elements (in order of power)
1. **Specific numbers**: "$47,329 in 30 days" beats "made a lot of money"
2. **Named case studies**: "Sarah Johnson, a real estate agent in Austin"
3. **Screenshots and evidence**: Show the receipts
4. **Third-party validation**: Press mentions, certifications, awards
5. **Demonstrations**: Show it working in real-time
6. **Credentials**: Years of experience, number of clients
7. **Guarantees**: Your confidence in the product

### Specificity = Believability
- Vague claims feel like lies
- Specific claims feel like truth
- "$47,329" is more believable than "$47,000" (exact number = real data)
- "In 31 days" is more believable than "in about a month"

## Transitional Elements for Better Flow

Copy should flow like a conversation, not read like a list. Use transitions to guide the reader:

- "Here's the thing..."
- "Now, you might be thinking..."
- "But that's not even the best part..."
- "Let me explain why this matters to you..."
- "And here's where it gets interesting..."
- "The truth is..."
- "So what does this mean for you?"
- "Now I know what you're thinking..."

## Framework for a 5-Page Sales Letter

1. **Page 1: The Hook and Problem**
   - Headline that stops them
   - Problem identification and agitation
   - "If you've ever [experienced problem], keep reading"

2. **Page 2: Your Story and Credibility**
   - Origin story (struggle -> discovery -> transformation)
   - Credentials and authority markers
   - Connection to their situation

3. **Page 3: The Solution and Mechanism**
   - Introduce your unique method/system
   - The "what without how" technique
   - Why this is different from everything else

4. **Page 4: Social Proof and Objection Handling**
   - Case studies and testimonials
   - FAQ addressing top objections
   - Risk reversal / guarantee

5. **Page 5: The Offer and Close**
   - Value stack
   - Pricing reveal
   - Urgency/scarcity
   - Final CTA
   - P.S. (often the second most-read element after the headline)
