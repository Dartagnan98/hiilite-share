# Headlines Framework

Source: Jeremy Haynes / Megalodon Marketing - Master Internet Marketing (Week 5 Copywriting Lessons)
Rooted in: Eugene Schwartz's "Breakthrough Advertising"

---

## 3 Questions to Ask Before Writing Any Headline

From Breakthrough Advertising by Eugene Schwartz, taught by Jeremy Haynes.

### Question 1: What Is the Mass Desire That Creates the Market?

Mass desire is an existing want inside the market you're trying to reach. You're tapping into it, not creating a new one.

- Don't pay additional money to create a new desire
- Think: what are the desires in my existing market?
- Use that to write and strengthen headlines
- If a headline doesn't have mass desire integrated, people won't respond

### Question 2: How Much Do These People Know About Your Product? (State of Awareness)

- Do they know your brand?
- Do they know the product category exists?
- Are they aware they have a problem but no solutions exist?
- Or are there already companies marketing solutions to them?
- This dictates the verbiage and approach in your headlines

### Question 3: How Many Products Have Been Presented Before Yours? (State of Sophistication)

- **Stage 1**: You can be direct -- "We've got this. Come buy it."
- **Stage 2**: Same type of headline, just outbid and outcompete
- **Stage 3**: Communicate the HOW -- how you produce the result
- **Stage 4** (hardest): Talk about the mechanism -- the process to getting the result

## 3 Steps in Developing a Headline

Use as a checklist after writing your headlines:

1. **Name the desire or its solution in the headline** -- Does it call out what they want?
2. **Prove that the solution can be accomplished** -- Does it feel achievable?
3. **Show that the mechanism of accomplishment is contained in your product** -- Does it connect to your offer?

Write a few headlines first, then check each one against these 3 criteria. Easier to tweak than to write perfectly from scratch.

## 5 Rules for Writing Great Headlines

### Rule 1: Get Self-Interest in Every Headline
- Not YOUR self-interest -- the MARKET's self-interest
- Incorporate mass desire into the headline
- "Make your headline suggest to the readers that there is something they want"
- Most marketers violate this rule

### Rule 2: If You Have News, Get It in the Headline
- New product, new use for old product, new breakthrough
- Study how news sites (Fox, CNN) write headlines that provoke interest
- Better used when communicating to a market that hasn't heard of you yet

### Rule 3: Avoid Headlines That Merely Provoke Curiosity
- Curiosity COMBINED with news or self-interest = excellent
- Curiosity by itself = seldom enough
- A well-crafted headline can inspire action on its own (more effective than entire body copy)
- Don't rely on curiosity alone -- that's clickbait

### Rule 4: Avoid Gloomy or Negative Headlines
- Take the cheerful and positive angle
- People want HOPE -- they want your product to pull them out of a problem
- It's good to leverage the downside in BODY COPY
- But in your HEADLINE: focus on the upside

### Rule 5: Suggest a Quick and Easy Way to Get What They Want
- Headline should feel like "this isn't going to be complex and hard"
- BUT don't over-imply the claim (compliance risk)
- Don't compare to something trivially easy (e.g., "as easy as putting something in the microwave" was noncompliant)
- Balance: accessible, not spammy

## The Simplicity Rule (Critical Tip)

> "Always, always, always simplify." -- Jeremy Haynes

**The Elon Musk / PayPal Example:**

BAD headline: "We have a tightly integrated financial infrastructure that can process all your banking needs under a single financial institution."

GOOD headline: "We can send money to your email address."

- Because you're an expert, you naturally overcomplicate
- Even if you reduce what you know to 10%, you're still more advanced than your audience
- Simplify your headlines AND body copy
- Write at a level where anyone can understand it instantly

## 38 Ways to Strengthen a Headline

From Eugene Schwartz's Breakthrough Advertising (Chapter 5-6). Use AFTER writing your initial headline to refine and supercharge it.

1. Measure the SIZE of the claim
2. Measure the SPEED of the claim
3. Compare the claim to its unnamed rival
4. Metaphorize the claim (create metaphors)
5. Sensitize the claim -- make the prospect feel, smell, touch, see, or hear it
   - VAK system (Richard Bandler): 35% visual, 25% auditory, 40%+ kinesthetic
   - Kinesthetic people operate off senses, feelings, emotion-based logic
6. Demonstrate the claim by showing a prime example
7. Dramatize the claim or its result
   - Example: "They laughed when I sat at the piano, but when I started to play..."
8. State the claim as a paradox
9. Remove limitations from the claim
10. Associate the claim with values/people the prospect wants to be identified with (affinity)
11. Show how much work and detail the claim does (good for Stage 3 sophistication)
12. State the claim as a question
    - "Who else wants whiter teeth, with no hard work?"
13. Offer information about how to accomplish the claim (Stage 3-4 sophistication)
14. Tie authority into the claim -- before AND after
15. Stress the NEWNESS of the claim
16. Stress the EXCLUSIVITY of the claim
17. Turn the claim into a CHALLENGE for the reader
18. State the claim as a case history quotation
19. Condense the claim
20. Interchange your product and the product it replaces
21. Symbolize the claim (replace direct statement with parallel reality)
22. Connect the mechanism of the claim in the headline (Stage 4 sophistication)
23. Startle the reader by contradicting how they think the mechanism should work (great for high skepticism)
24. Connect the need and the claim in the headline
25. Offer information in the ad itself
    - Example: "What everybody ought to know about the stock and bond business"
26. Turn the claim of the need into a case history
27. Give a NAME to the problem or need
28. Warn the reader about pitfalls if they don't use the product
29. Emphasize the claim by breaking it into two sentences or repeating part of it
30. Show how easy the claim is by imposing universally overcome limitations
31. State the DIFFERENCE in the headline
32. Surprise the reader -- former limitations have now been overcome
33. Address people who CAN'T buy your product (qualify)
34. Address your prospect directly
    - Example: "To the man who will settle for nothing less than the presidency of his firm"
35. Dramatize how hard it would be to produce the claim
36. Accuse the claim of being too good
    - "Is it immoral to make money this easily?"
37. Challenge the prospect's present limiting beliefs
    - "You're twice as smart as you think"
38. Turn the claim into a question and answer

## Headline Writing Rules (Quick Reference)

1. **Self-Interest First**: Every headline answers "what's in it for me?"
2. **Specificity Wins**: "Make $10,000/month" beats "Make more money." Numbers, timeframes, details always outperform vague claims
3. **Target the Right Audience**: Call out who you're talking to. If selling to dentists, say "dentists"
4. **Combine Elements**: Best headlines combine 2-3 categories. Example: Question + Number + Identity = "Are You Making These 5 Mistakes That Cost Real Estate Agents Thousands?"
5. **Test Everything**: Headlines are the single most impactful element. Test 5-10 variations minimum
6. **First Line = First Impression**: If the headline doesn't stop the scroll, nothing else matters
7. **Avoid Clickbait Without Substance**: Curiosity is powerful only when combined with self-interest or news
8. **Match Awareness Level**: Unaware audiences need education-first headlines. Aware audiences want specific claims and offers
