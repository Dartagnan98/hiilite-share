## The Virtuous Cycle of Premium Pricing

### Why You Should Charge More, Not Less

Hormozi's argument against competing on price:

"There is a very large, deep, dark, dangerous chasm between 'Cheap' and 'Premium' pricing. One in which many businesses die."

The commoditization trap:
- You lower price to compete
- Lower price = lower margins = less money for fulfillment
- Worse fulfillment = worse results = worse reputation
- Worse reputation = fewer customers = need to lower price more
- Death spiral

The premium pricing virtuous cycle:
- You charge premium prices
- Higher margins = more money for fulfillment
- Better fulfillment = better results = better reputation
- Better reputation = more referrals = can charge even more
- Growth spiral

## The Math of Premium Pricing

From the book, comparing two scenarios:

**Low-ticket scenario:**
- Price: $100/month
- Refund rate: 10%

- Refund rate: 10%

- Fulfillment cost: $200
- Profit per customer: $1,600
- Need 1 customer to make $1,600

"It is 160x more profitable per customer to charge premium."

## Emotional Investment Creates Better Clients

"People who pay, pay attention." When people invest significantly:
- They follow through more (less refunds from non-compliance)
- They get better results (because they actually do the work)
- They refer more (because they got results)
- They complain less (they respected the value going in)

## Price-to-Value Discrepancy

"The goal of a Grand Slam Offer is to create such a discrepancy between price and value that the prospect would be a fool to say no."

If your offer stacks up to $10,000 in perceived value and you charge $2,000, that's a 5:1 value-to-price ratio. The prospect thinks: "I'm getting $10K of stuff for $2K."

Target: 10:1 value-to-price ratio minimum.

"Price is what you pay. Value is what you get. Our job is to make value dwarf price."

## Low-Price to High-Price Migration

### Starting Free or Cheap to Build Flow

"Start with low prices or even free. The reason is that if you start there, you can go up. It's very difficult to come down."

Hormozi's personal example:
- First business: Online personal training
- Charged $0 -- asked clients to donate to charity of their choice ($500-$1,000)
- Got first 13 customers this way
- After 12 weeks of great results, asked: "Would you be willing to pay me now?"
- "Yeah, I'm the charity now"
- Used results and referrals to raise prices from there

### The Three-Phase Migration

1. **Phase 1: Free/charity/low-cost** -- Get testimonials, case studies, proof. Learn fulfillment.
2. **Phase 2: Market-rate pricing** -- Now you have proof. Charge what competitors charge.
3. **Phase 3: Premium pricing** -- Now you have superior proof. Charge 5-10x what competitors charge.

"Each phase funds the credibility needed for the next phase."

## Pricing Decision Framework

- **Cost-plus pricing** (BAD): "It costs me $X to deliver, so I'll charge $X + margin"
- **Market-based pricing** (MEDIOCRE): "Competitors charge $Y, so I'll charge slightly less/more"
- **Value-based pricing** (GOOD): "This delivers $Z in value to the customer, so I'll charge a fraction of $Z"

"Never compete on price. If the only difference between you and a competitor is price, you've already lost."

"Charge as much money for your products or services as humanly possible."

"If you are not embarrassed by your price, you're not charging enough."

"The commoditized business owner who tries to get more customers is doing so at the mercy of larger advertising budgets."

## Money Models: Maximizing Cash Flow from Pricing

### Definition

A money model is a deliberate sequence of offers structured to:
1. Generate the most upfront cash
2. Get the most leads to convert
3. Maximize lifetime value
4. Accelerate cash flow timing

"Most businesses spend money to get customers and then HOPE to make it back over time. That's playing on hard mode. Money Models flips that -- you make more money getting a customer than it costs to get them within the first 30 days."

### The Four Offer Types in a Money Model

**1. Attraction Offers** -- Maximize likelihood of conversion (getting leads to buy)
- Tend to have upfront cash components that pull cash flow forward
- Goal: get the most people through the door

**2. Upsells** -- Maximize gross profit per customer
- Once someone buys, what else can they buy?
- "People have different wallets" -- gym membership wallet, supplement wallet, apparel wallet, food wallet
- Each is a separate buying decision with separate budget

**3. Downsells** -- Turn nos into yeses
- Capture customers who won't pay for the premium but will pay for something
- CRITICAL: Must be presented so that people who would have bought the premium still do
- Bad downsell: cannibalizes your premium offer
- Good downsell: only captures incremental sales from people who were leaving

**4. Continuity** -- Get customers to buy again and again
- Transforms one-time transactions into ongoing relationships
- "We don't get customers to make sales. We make sales to get customers."

### Money Model Case Study: Gym Industry Disruption

**Competitor's model:**
- $21 for 21-day trial (attraction offer)
- 25% conversion of 100 leads = 25 customers x $21 = $525 upfront
- 35% convert to $99/month membership (continuity) = 8 customers
- 8 customers x 5-month average retention x $99 = $3,960
- Total from 100 leads: $525 + $3,960 = ~$5,325
- Max spend per lead: $53
- Problem: Takes 5+ months to recoup ad spend

**Hormozi's model:**
- Free 6-week challenge (attraction offer -- gets more leads at lower cost)
- 15% conversion at $500 upfront = 15 customers x $500 = $7,500 (day 1)
- Supplement upsell: 12 of 15 buy $200 avg = $2,400 (day 1-2)
- Rollover upsell: Credit $500 toward annual membership at $200/month = 70% take rate = 10 customers
- 10 customers x 5 months x $200 = $10,000 (over 5 months)
- Prepay discount at week 6: 20% of remaining buy annual = ~$5,000 (week 6)
- Total from 100 leads: $7,500 + $2,400 + $10,000 + $5,000 = $24,900
- Max spend per lead: $249
- Result: **4.7x more revenue from the same 100 leads**
- Almost all cash collected in first 30 days

"The guys I was competing against sold the same product as me. Maybe my trainers were better, maybe my location was a little better. But fundamentally, I won because I had a better money model."

### Money Model Mechanics (Specific Offer Types Used)

From the gym case study, four named mechanics:
1. **Win-your-money-back offer** (attraction) -- Free/low-cost entry that front-loads a high-ticket sale at the point of maximum motivation
2. **You-can't-buy-X-without-Y upsell** (classic upsell) -- Supplements immediately after gym purchase, hitting a different wallet
3. **Rollover upsell** (upsell + continuity) -- Crediting money already paid toward a longer commitment ("Let me put your $500 toward the annual")
4. **Buy-X-get-Y prepay** (continuity acceleration) -- "Buy 10 months, get 2 free" to lock in cash and extend retention

### Key Money Model Principles

1. **Front-load cash collection** -- Get as much money as possible in the first 48 hours
2. **Different wallets** -- People have separate mental budgets for different categories. Sell into each wallet.
3. **Credit rollover** -- Use money already paid as leverage for the next offer ("Let me credit your $500 toward the annual")
4. **The business that can spend the most to acquire a customer wins** -- Money models are about maximizing this number
5. **Stack offer types** -- Use multiple attraction, upsell, downsell, and continuity offers in sequence
6. **Outspend competitors into oblivion** -- "In an auction of attention, the person who can outspend has an ethical and legal monopoly of attention"
7. **Point of greatest pain = point of greatest sale** -- "When someone signs up, their highest point of motivation is the moment they walk in the door. That is when they're the most deprived of the outcome. That is the point I'm not trying to sell someone something for $20. I'm trying to sell them something for $500."

## PRICING PLAYBOOKS (Deep Pass)

### Anchor Upsell Playbook
- Learned from suit shop: budgeted $500, saw $16,000 Armani suit first, ended up spending $2,500-3,000
- Rolls-Royce stopped showing at car shows, started showing at plane/yacht shows
- Anchor must be 5-10x the price of main offer (not 20% more)
- Steps: Present anchor -> Get the gasp -> Come to rescue -> Present main offer -> Ask for payment
- If they DON'T gasp: close the whale
- One out of 10 buying 10x product = doubles your business
- Change only secondary features between anchor and main offer (keep primary features the same)

### The Gasp Test
- "If you didn't get a gasp from the price, you didn't go high enough"
- A certain percentage of population ALWAYS buys the most expensive thing
- "Put it there with the expectation you're never going to sell and be surprised when you do"

### Skip-and-Anchor Method for Raising Prices
1. Want to go from $2K to $3K? Anchor at $4K (skip target price)
2. Sales team presents $4K as new price
3. "But you can prepay today for $3K" (the actual target)
4. Sales team still has $2K as emotional home base
5. Always clear the pipeline: announce price increase to create urgency before the change

### Price Increase Letter Format (Existing Customers)
1. Set the stage: "You've noticed we've been growing"
2. What we're currently doing (re-sell them on forgotten value)
3. What we want to do in the future (ambitious improvements)
4. Tie to values: "With integrity, I cannot maintain my promise unless we do this"
5. Communicate price in smallest unit (daily or weekly, not annual)
6. PS: "If this will materially affect your life, reach out"
- Execution: video AND direct mail, turn comments off

### Supply Control Pricing
- Chanel model: 1-2 bags per store, every bag is limited edition
- Create waiting list even if you're not at capacity
- Periodically "let off the steam" to capture demand at peak pricing
- "Being at full capacity is completely up to you -- you can say today that you are full"
- Marketing shifts the demand curve: more people know about you = higher prices

### Close Rate Diagnostics
- 60-80% = 3-5x price increase sitting there
- 50-60% = 2-3x available
- 30-50% = appropriately priced
- Below 30% = selling skill issue

### Pricing Architecture
1. 10x/50x/100x rule: create something at 10x, 50x, or 100x of base price
2. Price anchor high: show most expensive first
3. Medium price placement: $5/$9/$10 structure drives people to premium (not $5/$6/$10)
4. Match buying personas: $100/$200/$1000 (different people), not $100/$110/$120 (same person)
5. Value-based pricing: same service, different price based on customer revenue
6. Expect 1 in 20 to buy top tier

### Pricing Delivery Hack
- Before stating price: "Before I tell you the price, it's super expensive"
- Rich: think their version of expensive, your number seems fine
- Poor: brace themselves, less sticker shock
- Other: write price on paper, slide it. Or use calculator, turn screen to them

### Price Increase Raises Close Rates
- 50% price increase on health company -> close rates went UP
- "They were so cheap compared to the promise that people didn't even believe it worked"
- Wine experiment: same wine rated differently based on price label

## PRICING INSIGHTS (Batch 7)

### "They Can't Afford It" Is Always Wrong
- Ferrari test: If selling a brand new Ferrari for $10K, people would call friends, family, max credit cards, take loans
- "The reason someone is not buying is simply because they do not understand the value"
- "Everyone can afford it if they believe the value is there"

### Price Anchoring: The Gasp Test (Expanded)
- "If you didn't get a gasp from the price, you didn't go high enough"
- The gasp shows you've broken the "rubber band of pricing" they associated with your product
- A certain percentage of population ALWAYS buys the most expensive thing
- "Put it there with the expectation you're never going to sell and be surprised when you do"

### Frozen Yogurt Psychology Playbook (Applicable to Any Business)
1. **Self-serve pricing by usage:** Customers blame themselves, not you
2. **Remove small option:** Only offer medium and large (power of default) -- increased sales 20-30%
3. **Present highest-margin items first:** Yogurt (highest margin) -> dry toppings -> fresh fruit (lowest margin)
4. **Buy foreclosed equipment:** rasmus.com, 5-10 cents on the dollar
5. **Competitions for customer acquisition:** Fraternities, companies, affiliations competing against each other
6. **Text list capture:** "Save 50% today by joining" = $2 lead acquisition

### Career Year Pricing (Hotline Example)
- $30K ask, downsell to $15K down + $15K when kid gets job
- Credit the $4K workshop toward the $30K
- Pitch parents at "halfway call" (week 2-3): make it feel like phase 2 of existing process, not a new sale
- "You're going to split it with your kid -- they're becoming an adult now"
- Get third-party financing or do in-house payment plans

## PRICING FROM TOP DOWN (Deep Pass -- Batch 4)

### Tesla Roadster Approach
1. Start highest price first
2. Brand narrative works top-down: "Many couldn't afford this, so I made something more affordable"
3. Reverse doesn't work (budget brand going premium)
4. Fewer customers at high price = manageable operations
5. Add tiers one at a time, 5-10x each
6. Alex's tiers: $99/mo (School) -> $5K (L1) -> ~$35K (L2) -> $135K (L3) -> Portfolio (equity)

### Pareto on Steroids -- Upsell Math
- 20% of customers = 80% of profits
- 4% of customers = 64% of profits
- 1% of customers = 51% of profits
- Upsell rule: **5-10x price** per tier, expect **20% take rate**
- Example: 1,000 at $10/mo ($10K) + 200 at $100/mo ($20K) + 40 at $1,000/mo ($40K) + 8 at $10,000/mo ($80K) = $150K total from $10K base
- 15x revenue from adding 3 tiers to a base product

### Suffering Is a Fixed Cost
- Poverty is tough. Growth is tough. Being an employee is tough. Being rich is tough
- "What if all three paths have the same amount of suffering? Pick the biggest reward"
- "The goal is to reframe reality so that bad things are good, not to only experience good things"

### Passion = Suffering, Etymology
- Latin "passio" = suffering. First usage: Passion of Christ (crucifixion)
- 95% of daily work won't be "your passion" even in a passion-based business
- "You're using the excuse of a lack of passion to disguise your inability to handle difficulty"

## FROM $100M LOST CHAPTERS

### Pick Your Price Model (from Book)

**Two versions:**

**Version 1 (Value Ladder Pick Your Price):**
- Present multiple tiers with increasing value at increasing prices
- Each tier includes a guarantee
- Let the customer self-select based on perceived value and budget
- Example tiers: $0 (basic access), $50 (standard + guarantee), $200 (premium + VIP + guarantee)

**Version 2 (Supporter Labels):**
- Same product at every level, different identity labels
- Example: $0 (freeloader), $10 (supporter), $50 (champion), $100 (hero)
- Appeals to identity, status, and social belonging
- "Nobody wants to be labeled a freeloader"

**COVID Car Wash Story:**
- During COVID, car wash implemented pick-your-price
- Average ticket: **$99** (normal price was significantly lower)
- Massive goodwill, customer loyalty through the roof
- "When you trust people to pay what it's worth, most people pay MORE"

**Key insight:** Pick Your Price works best when there's already established goodwill or when the product has clear, demonstrable value. It fails when used as a desperation move or when value isn't obvious.
