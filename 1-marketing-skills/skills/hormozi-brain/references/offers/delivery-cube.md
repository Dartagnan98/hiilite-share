## Source: Alex Hormozi ($100M Offers book + 383 video transcripts)

## The 6 Cheat Code Dimensions

For each solution in your offer, decide HOW you'll deliver it using these 6 dimensions:

### 1. Ratio -- 1:1, Small Group, or 1:Many?

- 1:1 personal training vs. group classes vs. app-based
- 1:1 is highest perceived value, 1:many is highest margin
- Mix ratios: use 1:1 for onboarding/key moments, 1:many for routine delivery

### 2. Effort Level -- DIY, Done-With-You, or Done-For-You?

- **DIY:** Give them the meal plan (lowest cost, lowest perceived value)
- **DWY:** Cook with them on a weekly video call (middle)
- **DFY:** Deliver pre-made meals to their door (highest cost, highest perceived value)

The more you do for them, the more you can charge. "Done-for-you" is always worth more than "do-it-yourself."

### 3. Medium -- In-Person, Phone/Video, or Digital/Recorded?

- Live in-person has highest perceived value
- Recorded/digital has lowest marginal cost
- Mix high-touch for key moments with scalable for routine delivery

### 4. Support Channel -- Text, Email, Chat, Community, Phone?

- More access = higher perceived value
- "Having a coach you can text anytime" scores much higher than "email support within 48 hours"
- Stack channels: community for peer support, text/phone for coach access

### 5. Speed & Convenience -- How Fast Do They Get It? How Convenient Is Consumption?

- Same-day access vs. wait for next cohort
- On-demand vs. scheduled
- "If I can get it now, it's worth more than waiting 30 days"

### 6. The 10x/1:10th Test

- Can you deliver 10x the value?
- Can you deliver at 1/10th the cost?
- For every delivery vehicle, ask both questions
- This forces creative thinking about efficiency and value multiplication

## Enhancement vs. Core Offer

The Delivery Cube helps you design both:

**Core offer:** The primary thing they're buying (the training program, the consulting engagement, the service)

**Enhancements:** Additional delivery vehicles that increase perceived value without proportionally increasing cost

Example for a fitness business:
- Core: 12-week training program (1:many, digital, DWY)
- Enhancement 1: Private community (1:many, digital, peer support)
- Enhancement 2: Weekly group Q&A call (small group, video, DWY)
- Enhancement 3: Personal coach text access (1:1, text, high-touch)
- Enhancement 4: Custom meal plan (1:1, digital, DFY)

Each enhancement moves the needle on a different value driver without requiring proportional cost increases.

## Commoditized vs. Decommoditized Delivery

**Commoditized delivery:** Everyone delivers the same way
- Personal training: 1:1, in-person, 3x/week
- Consulting: 1:1, phone call, monthly retainer
- Web design: DFY, digital, project-based

**Decommoditized delivery:** Unique combination nobody else offers
- Personal training + pre-made meals + daily text coaching + private community + body scan tracking
- Consulting + implementation team + weekly sprints + Slack channel + live dashboard
- Web design + conversion tracking + A/B testing + monthly optimization + revenue guarantee

"When you name your system, you own it. Nobody can comparison shop 'The 3-Phase Metabolism Reset Protocol' because nobody else has it. They can comparison shop 'personal training' because every gym offers it."

## Supply and Demand Manipulation Through Delivery

"If you can increase demand while decreasing supply (or perceived supply), price goes up infinitely."

Tactics:
- **Increase demand:** Better marketing, better offer, more channels
- **Decrease supply:** Limit spots, limit availability, increase exclusivity
- **Both simultaneously:** "We only take 10 clients per quarter" (scarcity) + "We have a waitlist of 50 people" (social proof of demand)

## Commoditization vs. Decommoditization Tactics

### What Makes Something a Commodity

A commodity is something that is:
- Undifferentiated from competitors
- Compared solely on price
- Interchangeable (one provider is as good as another)

Examples: "Personal training," "web design," "social media management," "consulting"

### How to Decommoditize

You decommoditize by creating an offer that cannot be compared to anything else. When there's nothing to compare it to, there's no price anchor.

Tactics for decommoditization:
1. **Niche specificity** -- "Weight loss for postpartum women with diastasis recti" vs. "weight loss"
2. **Unique mechanism** -- Name your proprietary method ("The 3-Phase Metabolism Reset Protocol")
3. **Unique delivery** -- Combine elements nobody else combines
4. **Unique guarantee** -- A guarantee nobody else would dare offer
5. **Unique bonuses** -- Include things nobody else includes
6. **Unique name** -- A named offer feels like a product, not a service

## The Sales-to-Fulfillment Continuum in Delivery Design

"The easiest thing in the world to sell is the hardest thing to fulfill. The hardest thing to sell is the easiest thing to fulfill."

Examples:
- **Easy to sell, hard to fulfill:** "I'll do everything for you and guarantee you lose 30 lbs in 30 days or your money back." Very compelling, very difficult to deliver.
- **Hard to sell, easy to fulfill:** "Here's a PDF about nutrition." Easy to deliver, nobody wants to pay for it.

The sweet spot: Start with an offer that is easy to sell (even if harder to fulfill). Get customers flowing in. Once you have cash flow, optimize fulfillment. Gradually add operational structure without killing the offer.

"Most people make the mistake of trying to build the perfect fulfillment system before they have any customers. Start with the offer. Get customers. Then fix fulfillment."

### Divergent vs. Convergent Thinking in Delivery Design

- **Divergent thinking** (brainstorm phase): Generate as many delivery vehicle ideas as possible. No judgment. Quantity over quality.
- **Convergent thinking** (trim phase): Now filter ruthlessly. Pick only the best. Trim everything else.

"Use divergent thinking when brainstorming solutions. Use convergent thinking when building the final offer."
