## THE 4-PART INVESTMENT LENS

Evaluate every deal through four dimensions:

### 1. Yield
- How much cash flow comes in monthly?
- Hard money lending example: 4%/month = $100K/month on $2.5M = 48-96% annualized

### 2. Capital Preservation
- How likely is my money coming back?
- Startup = probably not getting money back
- Real estate with 2x collateral and personal guarantees = high preservation

### 3. Tax Advantage
- Will I get taxed at income or capital gains rates?
- Hard money lending = regular income (bad) -- 50% tax rate
- Equity appreciation = tax-free until sold (good)

### 4. Equity Growth
- Does the thing I'm buying become more valuable over time?
- Zero participation in upside = bad deal
- Owning equity in a growing business = compounding wealth

### Bad Deal Example (Wealth Advisor Pitch)
- $450K to a startup, 10% return at end of 12 months ($45K)
- Capital preservation: startup = probably not getting money back
- Yield: 10% (below S&P at ~20% that year)
- Tax: treated as income, effective return after taxes = 5.5%
- Equity growth: zero participation in upside
- Verdict: "Borderline unethical"

### Good Deal Example (Bridge Loan / Hard Money)
- $2.5M note to secure building out of foreclosure
- Building worth $5.1-5.6M (4 offers already, top buyer had $300K earnest money down)
- Personal guarantees from both buyers (net worths >$2.5M each)
- Yield: 4%/month = $100K/month. Doubles to 8% after month 4
- Capital preservation: building worth 2x+ the loan, personal guarantees, multiple backup buyers, first-position title lien
- Minimum carry: 2 months = $200K guaranteed even if deal closes in 24 hours
- Tax: treated as income (bad), but 27% after-tax return still makes sense
- Closed in 100+ days, total return: ~$350K (check back for $2.85M)
- After taxes (50% regular income): ~$175K net profit

## BUSINESS EVALUATION CRITERIA

### Green Flags (What Makes Hormozi Say Yes)

**Revenue Quality:**
- Recurring revenue (8-15x EBITDA multiple vs 4x for transactional)
- High gross margins (80%+ for service, 90%+ ideal)
- Net negative churn (referral % > churn % = infinite organic growth)
- LTV:CAC ratio of 3:1+ (automated) to 12:1+ (people-driven)

**Operational Quality:**
- Business can run without the founder (team-run, not owner-dependent)
- Three superstars in place: Acquisition, Delivery, Operations
- Multiple acquisition channels (not dependent on one platform)
- Clean unit economics at the location/unit level

**Market Quality:**
- Large TAM (total addressable market)
- Favorable supply/demand economics (growing demand outpacing new entrants)
- "The bloodier the water, the more fish are there" -- red oceans have biggest rewards
- Body-based services resist AI disruption (nail salons, dentists, haircuts, personal training)

**Founder Quality:**
- Coachable, willing to follow proven playbooks
- Not spread across multiple businesses
- Has proven the model works at small scale
- Character > cleverness: "You only get one name. You only get one reputation."

### Red Flags (What Makes Hormozi Say No)

**Revenue Problems:**
- Transactional revenue only (no recurring component)
- Below 80% gross margin
- High churn (>9% monthly in service businesses)
- Single acquisition channel dependency
- Revenue declining while owner claims to be working hard

**Operational Problems:**
- Owner IS the business (no one would buy it because it's just the owner with helpers)
- Trophy business: "Business is a trophy, not a vehicle for wealth" ($1.7M revenue, 6.4% margins)
- 500-item menu syndrome (trying to do too much, can't excel at anything)
- "Is your goal status or is your goal money?"

**Market Problems:**
- Shrinking TAM
- Too niche without room to expand
- Government regulatory risk (cannabis seed business example)
- Business model dependent on a single platform that could change rules

**Founder Problems:**
- Won't raise prices despite 60-80% close rates
- Multiple businesses, none profitable
- "I need a partner" when they really need to focus
- Entrepreneurs (vs intrapreneurs) -- "either they lose their juju or they take your business with them"

### Qualification Criteria (Gym Launch Example)
After implementing qualification filters (25+ clients, signed lease, full-time, ideally 1+ employee):
- Sales dropped from 100+/month to 40-50/month (cut in half)
- Escalations dropped from 22 to ZERO in first 4 weeks
- Qualified customers paid MORE than unqualified ones
- "Recipe for a higher profit business, happier customers, and better reputation"
- "Dear God, why did this take us three years?"

## DEAL STRUCTURES HORMOZI USES

### 1. Equity Investment (Primary Model)
- Take minority or majority equity stake
- Provide operational support, playbooks, pricing strategy
- Example: Enchanted Fairies -- killed the agency model, owned all locations directly. Founder made LESS for 9 months. Month 10: matched. 30 months: $2.5M/month with 30+ locations

### 2. Hard Money Lending
- Short-term, high-interest bridge loans secured by real assets
- $2.5M at 4%/month ($100K/month), doubling to 8% after month 4
- Defense layers: personal guarantees, first-position lien, multiple backup buyers, earnest money deposits
- Returns: 48-96% annualized yield
- Tax treatment: regular income (not capital gains) -- 50% tax rate
- "Trust is the most valuable thing you can have"

### 3. Licensing / Royalties
- Royalties come off the TOP (revenue share > profit share)
- "Somebody can play around with their profit. Top line is topline."
- Gym Launch was essentially a licensing model -- sold the system, nearly 100% margin
- Revenue share always better than profit share for the licensor

### 4. Majority Sale to Private Equity
- Sold 66% of Gym Launch + Prestige Labs for $46.2M to American Pacific Group (December 2021)
- Still owns 30%+ of Gym Launch
- Pre-COVID valuation was ~$150M
- Gym Launch grew 40% after Alex and Leila exited majority share under CEO Kale

### 5. Rollup Strategy (Prescribed to Others)
- Dentist association: bundle 10 practices/year, sell each bundle for $100M, keep 20%, give 80% to dentists
- Then sell the "rollup machine" itself at 10x its $20-30M/year profit = $200-300M exit
- vs. original one-time $25M exit
- PE firm with $35M portfolio: go vertical (same type = rollup). Buy 10 things for $1M each, sell all 10 together for $50M

### 6. Seller Financing / No-Money-Down Acquisitions
- Find businesses doing $250K+/year profit where owner is tired
- Agree on price first, THEN negotiate terms
- Get seller financing for 75% of the deal
- Friend combined four ~$1M/year info businesses: "Give it to me, I'll pay you the income for the next 2 years." Owned the $5M asset for nothing out of pocket

### 7. Profit Sharing + Equity Buy-In (Billionaire's Model)
- CEO gets 5% of profits + opportunity to buy increasing equity chunks over time
- One CEO started at 5-10% ownership, now owns ~30% of the largest company
- "Not being greedy grows the pie faster than keeping a bigger slice"

### 8. Insurance / Franchisor Model
- Get paid before delivering, may never need to deliver
- Predates the tax code. Oldest companies (180-300+ years) are insurance companies
- Franchisors: "get paid and then remit the remainder" -- control the money flow

## EBITDA MULTIPLES BY BUSINESS TYPE

### Transactional Businesses
- $10M revenue, $3M EBITDA = **4x multiple** = $12M enterprise value
- One-time purchases, no recurring component
- "Transactional business that hits $5M? Just worth the income"

### Recurring Revenue Businesses
- $10M revenue, $3M EBITDA = **8-15x multiple** = $24M-$45M enterprise value
- Netflix (fixed membership) vs Coca-Cola (habitual purchasing) -- both count as recurring/reoccurring

### Owner-Dependent vs Team-Run
- Owner-dependent $5M business = worth the income only (nobody would buy it)
- Team-run $5M business ($2M take-home, doesn't require owner) = $10-20M asset
- "You become wealthy from the things you own, not the things you do"

### Impact of Growth on Enterprise Value
- Going from $2M to $3M annual EBITDA = adding $10M+ to net worth (at 10x multiple)
- At S&P-level multiples (40x): $2M EBITDA increase = $80M net worth increase
- "What you build builds your net worth, not what you make"

### Specific Examples
- Gym Launch: sold at implied ~$70M total valuation (66% for $46.2M)
- Panda Express: $3.7B sales, 27% net margins, $935M distributions, 100% owner-operated (no franchises)
- Enchanted Fairies: grew to $2.5M/month ($30M/year run rate) in 30 months from agency pivot
- Dr. John Berardi / Precision Nutrition: 9-figure+ exit

## ENTERPRISE VALUE FRAMEWORKS

### What Drives Enterprise Value
Enterprise value = discount applied to future cash flows = how much money x how likely it will occur
- Lots of likely future money = big multiple
- Little uncertain money = small/no multiple

### How to Increase Enterprise Value
1. **Switch from transactional to recurring** (doubles or triples the multiple)
2. **Remove owner dependency** (makes the business sellable at all)
3. **Add multiple acquisition channels** (reduces risk, increases multiple)
4. **Improve gross margins** (80% -> 90% = 2x more profitable, 90% -> 95% = 2x again)
5. **Reduce churn** (10% -> 3% monthly = 3.3x LTV improvement)
6. **Build brand** (pricing power = Warren Buffett's "single most important decision in evaluating a business")

### The Continuity Premium
- Transactional $10M/$3M EBITDA = 4x = $12M
- Recurring $10M/$3M EBITDA = 8-15x = $24-45M
- Same revenue, same profit, 2-4x difference in what you can sell it for

### Billing Optimization for Enterprise Value
- Bill every 4 weeks (not monthly) = 13 cycles/year vs 12 = 8.3% more revenue
- For 20% profit business: ~41% increase in profit from this alone
- Add "3% processing fee" = straight to bottom line. For 10% profit business = 30% profit increase
- Get 2 forms of payment (card + ACH). ACH links to bank, rarely changes

## WHEN TO SELL VS HOLD

### Hormozi's Default: Hold
- "The wealthiest people in the world buy and build. Most times they don't sell."
- Warren Buffett model: See's Candy for 30+ years, Coca-Cola, GEICO
- Don't sell your business for an exit -- take loans against it
- $1M business grows to $2M: take $700K loan = tax-free cash, still own the business

### Reasons to Sell
- When a buyer values it significantly higher than you can grow it (American Pacific Group offered during market peak)
- When you've lost the fire for that specific business
- When a rollup opportunity makes the parts worth more as a whole
- When you need liquidity for a bigger play

### The Tax Argument for Holding
- 94% of Forbes 100 growth came from appreciation of equities, not income
- Selling triggers capital gains tax; holding lets appreciation compound tax-free
- Loans against appreciated assets = spendable money without triggering taxes
- "We're all taxed at 100% -- we don't get to keep it no matter what" (death)

### The "Don't Sell" Math
- Business at $10M EBITDA, 5x multiple = $50M valuation
- After-tax dividends at 37% rate: $6.3M/year
- Grow to $12M EBITDA: valuation goes to $60M, dividends $7.6M
- Net worth increase: $17.6M, effective tax rate only 21% (56% was tax-free appreciation)
- Selling would net far less after earnouts, transition periods, and taxes

### The Billionaire's Approach to Holding
- Man doing $1B+/year, 50 privately held companies, mid-70s
- "I don't believe businesses should make a profit. Profits are unnatural."
- How $100M -> $1B: "We gave up. Broke company into 100 pieces, gave equity to superstars, checked out. Five years later: $1 billion."
- Investment approach: multi-family real estate, never below 8 cap rate
- "More concerned with return OF capital than return ON capital"

## INVESTMENT PHILOSOPHY AND RULES

### Rule 1: Invest in Skills Before Markets
- Don't invest in stocks/real estate until money is flowing in FASTER than you can spend on skill acquisition
- $25K, $50K, $250K, $500K in the bank? Spend it ALL on acquiring skills
- Your business (where you have expertise) will almost always outperform passive investments
- "Somebody who has fewer skills needs more money"
- $1,000 in S&P = $1,100 at year end (10% return). $1,000 in sales training = $40K-$220K income increase = 18,000% return, every year going forward

### Rule 2: Framework Over Outcome
- Ray Dalio: "If I were to use the decision-making framework that would have made that deal a winner, I'd have to apply that same framework to all other situations that would have been losers, and my net net would cause me to lose"
- Applied to bitcoin: Buffett framework says don't buy speculative assets. Bitcoin went up. Still considers the decision correct
- "Don't change your swing every time you strike out"

### Rule 3: Wealth Comes from Appreciation, Not Income
- 94% of Forbes 100 growth = appreciation of equities, not income
- At 40x multiples: $2M EBITDA increase = $80M net worth increase
- Richest people live in highest-tax states (CA, NY) because they understand this
- "What you build builds your net worth, not what you make"

### Rule 4: Risk Management
- Jack Butcher: "You make fortunes by taking a lot of risk with a little bit of money. You maintain fortunes by taking small amounts of risk with a lot of money"
- Bezos: "Given a 10% chance of a 100x payoff, you should take that bet every time"
- Lynch: "A stock can only go to zero. But it can go infinitely high"
- "Baseball has truncated outcomes (max 4 runs). In business, you can score a thousand runs."
- Never bet the farm: "Don't put everything on black when you cannot afford to lose"

### Rule 5: Volatility Is Not Risk
- Insurance company example: loses money 1 out of 9 years (catastrophe years), makes a lot the other 8
- "My insurance business is not risky. It is volatile. Those are not the same things."
- Salespeople have volatile income but are LOW risk employees (they generate revenue)
- "If you want to decrease the volatility in your life, decrease your expenses to your salary"

### Rule 6: Wealth Is a Ratio
- "Wealth is a ratio between how much you spend versus how much you make"
- Salesperson making $140K living on $25K = 6x income-to-expense ratio
- Saving $115K/year at 10% returns = could technically retire in 2 years
- "A salesperson making $200K/year owns a million-dollar business. It's the same thing." (at 20% net margins)

### Rule 7: The Opportunity Cost Test
- Every dollar invested in the stock market is a dollar NOT invested in your skills/business
- Be "petrified" of investing in areas where people with deeper talent stacks will crush you
- Peter Lynch compounded at 30% for 15+ years -- but he was the BEST stock picker in history
- For most people: reinvest in what you're already world-class at

## BUSINESS SELECTION CRITERIA ("What Business to Start")

### The Perfect Business Model: UESA
- **U**nique -- Big competitive moat
- **E**xpensive -- Sells for a dollar, costs a penny (99% gross margin ideal)
- **S**ticky -- Habit-forming, high LTV
- **A**ir -- Digital/weightless, costs nothing to deliver
- Buffett: "Costs a penny, sells for a buck, habit-forming"

### The Three Ps
1. **Pain** -- Something you overcame personally
2. **Profession** -- What you do/did for work
3. **Passion** -- What you can't get enough of

### Opportunity Vehicle Assessment (Richard Schwarzman's Framework)
Three factors for pursuing any opportunity:
1. Really big potential (TAM)
2. Can be run by 10/10 talent
3. Makes economic sense (unit economics work)

### Four Business Shapes
1. **E-commerce**: Fast growth, hits breaking points (cash, traffic, distribution). "No gigantic businesses are drop shipping."
2. **Service**: Linear growth tied to headcount. Best for starting out.
3. **Software/SaaS**: Slow start, exponential once product-market fit achieved. Highest ceiling.
4. **Content/Media**: Slowest start. Years to build. Once working, ~100% margin.
- Buffett: "It's not as important how hard you row but what boat you are in"

### Business Growth Stage Benchmarks
- **$0-$1M**: One product, one channel. Reliable customer acquisition.
- **$1M-$10M**: Multi-products (cross-sells, upsells), sometimes multi-channel. 4-5x LTV.
- **$10M-$30M**: Professionalize (HR, legal, IT, accounting, compliance). You become a target.
- **$30M-$100M+**: Hub-and-spoke. Central ops with profit centers. Each run by an intrapreneur.

### Leverage Career Progression (Hormozi's Personal Path)
- Employee: 4 figures/month
- Self-employed: 5 figures/month
- Employer (gyms + turnarounds): 6 figures/month
- Licensing (content leverage): 7 figures/month
- Acquisition.com (capital + collaboration + content): 8 figures/month

### The $100M Blueprint
1. Get impossibly good at ONE thing (until people talk about you before you enter the room)
2. Monetize teaching that skill (high margin)
3. Reinvest profits into scalable asset (software, product, franchise)
4. Grow and exit (or hold forever)

### Body Services Are AI-Proof
- "Start a boring service business around the body" if starting from zero
- Cocoa Fit tried automated personal training machines -- went out of business
- "People hire trainers for human connection and accountability"
- Nail salons, dentists, haircuts, personal training: "People value the human exchange"

## TAX STRATEGY FOR INVESTORS

### The Big Rocks (What Actually Matters)
1. **Where you live**: Zero-tax state vs California (15-16%). Puerto Rico = 4% federal (6+ months)
2. **How you buy**: Buy $100 asset for $30 = instant 3x gain, untaxed. Compounds tax-free
3. **How/if you sell**: Don't sell. Hold forever. Buffett model.
4. **Live off loans**: Loans aren't income. Borrow against growth. As long as loans < growth = compound tax-free forever

### Tax Shelters Are Usually Not Worth It
- Conservation easements, captive insurance = "BBs" -- tiny impact
- IRS is 3-5 years behind. Most loopholes get unwound. Then fines + interest + legal
- Charlie Munger: "95% of tax solutions are swampland"
- Friend traveled 104 days/year for Puerto Rico tax residency: "Could you make 30% more if you didn't travel 104 days?" He hadn't thought about it
- Another friend missed their baby's birth for tax residency days
- Close relative got audited over captive insurance structure
- Hormozi spent $1.6M in legal/accounting fees, paid $22.68M in taxes over 5 years
- Could have saved ~$6M with an IDGT (intentionally defective grantor trust) but chose not to

### Seven Tax Beliefs
1. Attention is #1 most valuable asset. Never trade it for minor tax savings
2. Pay zero attention to tax savings schemes. Focus on making, not saving
3. Orders of magnitude thinking (10x, not save 10%)
4. Never dwell on what you would have saved on roads not taken
5. We're ALL taxed at 100% when we die -- chips stay on the table
6. Money exists to buy options. Don't trade optionality for money
7. "I'd prefer to be known as the person who paid his dues and still killed it"

### The 85-Year-Old Self Test for Tax/Investment Decisions
- "If I'm 85 years old, a decabillionaire, would that person care about saving 30% on taxes?"
- "Will I care if I have $9 billion or $11 billion when I die? No."
- "Don't sacrifice anything for me. I'm old. You're young. Enjoy."

## THE MONEY LADDER (Who Gets Paid When)

Restructure your business to be higher on the ladder:

1. **Employee**: Work first, paid 2-4 weeks later (lowest leverage)
2. **Self-employed**: Split (half now, half on delivery)
3. **Professional (doctor/surgeon)**: Paid first, then work
4. **Banks**: Preferred creditor, paid first if things go wrong
5. **Insurance**: Paid the whole time, may never deliver
6. **God/Franchisors**: 10% off the top, forever (highest leverage)

"You will be compensated in proportion to the risk you're willing to take."
"The people who do this well have it be very risky for other people and not risky at all for them."
