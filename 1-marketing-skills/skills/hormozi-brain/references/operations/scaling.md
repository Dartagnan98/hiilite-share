# Scaling
## Source: Alex Hormozi (383 video transcripts)

## The Pi Equation (Most Used Equation in Business)
- **Core formula**: Inflow / Outflow = Hypothetical Max
- Three applications:

### Application 1: Hypothetical Max Customers
- Inflow (new customers/month) / Churn rate = Max customers
- Example: 5 new customers/month / 10% churn = 50 max customers
- If currently at 30 with these rates, you're growing. If at 60, you're shrinking.

### Application 2: Lifetime Value (LTV)
- Average revenue per customer per month / Churn rate = LTV
- Example: $1,000/month / 20% churn = $5,000 LTV
- Gives you the number to market with (spend at least 1/3 of LTV to acquire)

### Application 3: Revenue Max
- New business $/month / Revenue churn rate = Revenue max
- Example: $10,000 new business/month / 10% churn = $100,000/month max

## The Golden Ratio (Net Negative Churn)
- Referral percentage > Churn percentage = infinite growth
- If 10% of customers refer monthly and 5% leave, business grows without paid advertising
- "This is the promised land"
- Product so good that organic growth exceeds churn

## Growth Curve Reality
- Growth follows a logarithmic curve approaching hypothetical max
- Rate of growth slows over time as churn eats bigger percentage
- Realistically cap at 80-90% of hypothetical max due to business volatility

## Inflow Is Your Bottleneck Framework

### The Math of Scaling (gym example)
- Want 300 members, 10% churn = need 30 new/month just to break even
- Converting 50% of trials = need 60 front-end signups/month
- 50% show rate = need 120 scheduled appointments/month
- That's 30/week, 5/day on 6-day selling schedule
- To GROW an extra 30: double everything above

### Acceptable Metrics
- Services = easy in, easy out (that's the territory)
- Churn below 1-2% is unlikely in service businesses
- "If you want to scale, your inflow never stops"
- Must sign up people AND fulfill concurrently

### Operational Preparation
- Block time: 2 hours/day for outbound (retention), 2 hours for onboarding, sales calls throughout
- Calculate cost: 15 leads/day at $10/lead = $150/day = $4,500/month
- "A lot of people don't confront the math"

### Emotional Readiness
- "Champions have short memories -- they bounce back fast"
- If you close 50%, half your meetings WILL say no
- "The most experienced sales people don't get bothered"

## The 20% Decrease Rule (Cost of Change)

### The Rule
- Every change in the business causes approximately a 20% decrease in performance
- Even if the change is superior, there's an immediate performance dip
- Team has muscle memory -- rebuilding takes time

### The Math
- If improvement only yields 5%, you took a guaranteed 20% loss for potential 5% gain
- Takes 4-5 months to make up the deficit
- Meanwhile, leaving people alone, they naturally improve ~5% on their own
- Net result: change cost you months for zero net gain

### The Decision Filter
- Only make changes with risk-adjusted 20%+ improvement
- Specifically: need 40% estimated improvement with 50/50 odds = 20% expected value
- "If I don't see a risk-adjusted 20% improvement, I make no change at all"

### Practical Application
- Keep "Alex's Big List of Ideas" on phone
- When you think of something, write it down instead of messaging the team
- If still thinking about it 2-4 weeks later, it has legs
- Team checks: "You have four other 50% return moves. Is this better than those?"
- "I have grown to accept that some shit stays shit"
- Most entrepreneurs change too many things too often -- never know what actually broke

## Punch Card Mentality (from Buffett)
- You have ~4 changes per quarter per department
- "If everybody had a punch card with 20 investments their whole life, people would do exceptionally well"
- Forces you to really think: "Is this the bet I'm going to make?"

## Relative vs Absolute Returns
- Spending more on ads: relative ROI goes down but absolute profit goes up
- $10K spend -> $100K return (10x, $90K profit) vs $1M spend -> $2M return (2x, $1M profit)
- Obviously take the $1M profit even at lower relative return

## Supply-Constrained Business Solution
1. Raise price (reduces demand, increases cash flow)
2. Use extra cash to advertise higher compensation
3. Attract talent needed to remove supply constraint
- "Usually there's two or three order consequences"

## SUPPLY-CONSTRAINED BUSINESS PLAYBOOK (Deep Pass -- Batch 3)

When you can't find enough producers/practitioners:
1. Reframe: you're in the RECRUITING business, not the service business
2. Create compelling career paths for the role
3. Use all four channels for recruiting: warm outreach, cold outreach, paid ads, organic content
4. Add headhunters/recruiters
5. Staff referral program with MASSIVE incentives ($20K-$100K per hire if they generate $350K/year)
6. As you get bigger, referral compounding becomes material

### Physical Therapy Example
- Each PT generates $350K gross profit/year
- "Why would you not pay $50K or $100K for a referral if you know they generate $350K?"
- Buddy M cleaning business: same reframe -- you're in the recruiting maids business

## ONBOARDING PLAYBOOK FOR LOW CONSUMPTION (Deep Pass -- Batch 3)

1. Closer books the onboarding call (BAMFAM -- book a meeting from a meeting)
2. 4-6 one-on-one sessions before group (adjust price if needed)
3. Each session ends with BAMFAM: pull up calendars, book next session
4. Automated reminder sequence + manual reach-out from session leader
5. Group sessions positioned as graduation/earned privilege
6. AB close: "Would you rather start one-on-one or in a group?"
   - If they choose group, gift them 1-2 one-on-one sessions anyway
- Disney stat: "It takes 37 magical moments to overcome one tragic moment"

## CONSTRAINT DIAGNOSIS FRAMEWORK (Deep Pass -- Batch 3)

Diagnostic sequence from quarterly meeting:
1. "Why do we expect it to be different this time?"
2. "Why weren't you able to do this?" -> lost reps, net zero
3. Turn to HR: "How many candidates per week?" -> 1 qualified/week
4. "How many interviews to get 1 qualified?" -> 5
5. "How many finals to get 1 hire?" -> 4
6. "What's churn?" -> 20%/quarter
7. Math reveals impossibility of hitting goal with current process

- "Every system will grow up to its constraint and no further"
- "What makes macro speed happen is knowing which problems to solve in what order"
- Result: $10-12M in missed annual revenue from solving the wrong problem for two quarters

## SCALING ADDITIONS (Batch 5-6)

### Nail It Then Scale It: Retention Math
- Company A: sells 100/year, keeps 100%. Year 2: 200. Year 3: 300
- Company B: sells 100/200/300 but loses 100/200/300 respectively
- Both look like 300 sales/year but B acquired 600 total vs A's 300
- "All of that added cost drops straight to the bottom line"
- "Advertising to acquire customers only becomes more expensive in time"

### Scaling Creates Lumpy Inefficiency (Expanded)
- New sales reps won't be as good as existing five
- New marketer won't be as good at content initially
- Business incurs full cost immediately but doesn't get return for months
- At 3:1 LTV:CAC with all manual processes, adding new people could push to unprofitable
- Need 12:1+ ratio buffer to absorb scaling inefficiency

### Local Business Expansion Tactics
1. Expand existing location (knock down walls, add seats, cut class times, extend hours)
2. Add more channels (Instagram + X + YouTube + Facebook + TikTok + etc.)
3. Open another location
- Most people's goals achievable within current vehicle -- too impatient

### Five Directions to Move Your Market
1. **Up market**: single gyms -> franchisers
2. **Down market**: gyms -> individual trainers (more people, less buying power)
3. **Adjacent**: gyms -> chiropractors
4. **Broader**: gyms -> health & wellness in general
5. **Narrower**: gyms -> only spin studios
