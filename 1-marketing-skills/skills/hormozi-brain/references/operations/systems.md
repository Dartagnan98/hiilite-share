## Simple Operational Framework (Activities + Time + Inflow)

### Step 1: List desired outcomes
- Think of business as pipeline: click -> opt-in -> schedule -> show -> buy -> resell
- Identify all activities required at each stage

### Step 2: Calculate time per activity per customer
- Example: 2 minutes/client/day for coaching check-ins
- At 100 clients, that's 3 hours 20 minutes/day

### Step 3: Block time on calendars
- Take activities + allocated time and place on each role's calendar
- This IS operations: activities, units of time, expected inflow
- "That's honestly all there is to it"

### Step 4: Calculate breaking points
- Know at what inflow level each role becomes overwhelmed
- Know when you need to create a new role

### Step 5: Time study for baseline
- Excel sheet with 15-minute increments
- Employee writes what they did each 15 minutes
- "You will be more efficient than when you do a time study. I promise."
- Reveals inefficiencies, padded roles, unnecessary activities

### Key insight on finding inefficiency:
- Many employees spend time on activities that aren't valuable
- Not malicious -- they just don't understand the bigger picture
- "A lot of people just don't know what's valuable"

## CEO to Owner Transition Framework

### The Identity Shift
- CEO = full-time role running the company
- Owner = strategic adviser, quarterly meetings, "nose in, hands out"
- NIHO principle: "Nose in, hands out" -- attend meetings but don't walk away with to-dos

### Litmus Test
- 6-month test: Does the company grow without your involvement?
- Not just maintain -- GROW in both top line and profit
- "The company has to be able to grow and innovate without you"

### Communication Structure
- Remove yourself from company Slack/Asana
- Create separate communication center for executives only
- Portfolio companies: one Slack with all executives, teams access their executives

### The Two-Step Forward Method (Active to Passive)
- Instead of taking one step forward (delegate current work, do next thing)...
- Take TWO steps forward: delegate current work AND get someone to do the next thing
- You step back to owner role while two people do both levels of work
- Your income may decrease but enterprise value increases

### Key Beliefs
- "You get paid on what you do, you get returns on what you own"
- Business that required you at $17M profit: not sellable (personality brand)
- Same business without you at $8M profit: far more valuable as enterprise
- "Poor people do the wrong things, rich people do the right things, wealthy people own the right things"

## Business That Runs Without You (5-Step Framework)

### Step 1: Self-Inventory
- List everything you do (literally all of it)
- Run a time study: 15-minute intervals, write one word for what you did
- "It'll be the most productive week of your life"
- Can do this with key employees too (key-man risk reduction)

### Step 2: Build Processes
- For each item: assign Project (one-time), Process (ongoing), or Person
- Color code: Green (can give to someone now), Yellow (need to build process first), Red (need person you don't have)
- Solve green first, then yellow, then red
- Create if-then decision trees for common scenarios
- Set approval thresholds (e.g., under $X, they can act independently)

### Step 3: Scorecards + KPIs
- Ask every person: "How do you in your role make the company money?"
- Tie every function to the bottom line
- Even ancillary roles: camera person -> bad work wastes team time, wastes footage, wrong equipment
- Reverse potential mistakes into measurable KPIs

### Step 4: Test and Graduate
- Shadow-train: they watch you do it
- Supervise: they do it in front of you
- Support independence: available but not involved
- "I'm cool with 80%, sometimes 60%, as long as I have a clear path to them getting better"
- Measure employees on rate of improvement, not starting skill level

### Step 5: Remove Yourself from Marketing
- 7 ways to generate marketing without founder's face:
  1. Screenshot customer praise from communities weekly -> ads
  2. Incentivize testimonials (unlock higher service tiers as reward)
  3. Lifecycle ads: pull recordings from sales call -> onboarding -> delivery -> testimonial
  4. Document key client deliverable moments (reveals, results, celebrations)
  5. Pay customer support $5 per mini-testimonial captured
  6. Turn Google/Yelp reviews into marketing (including funny 1-stars)
  7. Build affiliate program / TikTok shop

### The Phone Test
- "Can you take 3 months off and have the business grow?"
- If your phone doesn't ring and business is bigger when you return: you have a real business
- Overexpansion is #1 reason people go out of business

### Key Beliefs to Adopt
- "If I can do it right with part of my time, someone else can do it better with all of their time"
- "You can outwork anyone in your business. You will not be able to outwork everyone."
- A players are worth 5x B players (Harvard study)

## The What-How-Who Framework (Scaling Yourself)
*From Andy Grove's "High Output Management"*

### Three Questions When Someone Isn't Performing
1. **Do they know WHAT I want them to do?** (Communication)
   - Rule: "If it's not written, it never happened"
   - Follow up verbal directives with Slack/email/Asana
2. **Do they know HOW to do it?** (Training)
   - Provide training, course, hop on a call, have another employee train them
3. **Are they MOTIVATED to do it?** (Motivation)
   - Sub-reasons for lack of motivation:
     - Don't understand WHY it's important in larger company context
     - Feel like their work doesn't matter (meaningless/busy work)
     - Not bought into company vision/mission
   - "When you scale, what you scale is culture and training"

### Diagnosing Missing Elements
- Has WHAT + motivation but not HOW: pretends to be busy (ashamed they don't know)
- Has HOW + motivation but not WHAT: executes on irrelevant things, not aligned with priorities
- Has WHAT + HOW but not motivation: checks out, avoids confrontation, probably job hunting

## The Talent Grid (What-How-Who Applied to Departments)

### Three Levels of Competence

| Level | Focus | Role | Activity |
|-------|-------|------|----------|
| 1 | WHAT to do | Individual Contributor | Doing |
| 2 | HOW to do it | Manager | Teaching |
| 3 | WHO can do it | Leader | Systematizing |

### Level 1: Individual Contributor (What)
- You have to get things done yourself
- All time spent doing
- Developing base skills, becoming proficient

### Level 2: Manager (How)
- Understand the sequence of how things work
- Can teach others to replicate the skill
- Hold them accountable to consistent execution
- Note: managers don't need to be better than individual contributors at the task

### Level 3: Leader (Who)
- "Building the machine that builds the machine"
- First you do it, then you teach it, then you build something that teaches it for you
- This is what cracks open $1M+/month level
- Systematizing, not just delegating

### Department Grading
- Apply the grid to each department (marketing, sales, product, shared services)
- Grade yourself: where am I involved?
- Goal: move above all departments as CEO with leaders who build machines
- "If I'm not on the Slack channel... that is what I believe is a truly delegated business"
- Your strengths often become the bottleneck (you're comfortable delegating what you don't know)

### Key Insight from Reid Hoffman
- Most employees struggle to jump between levels
- Rapid-growth companies often change out leadership
- Good individual contributors are often terrible leaders (and vice versa)
- Takes a unique person to master all levels (why being an operator is so hard)

## Maker vs Manager Time

### Manager Schedule
- Divide time into smallest chunks possible (15 min to 90 min)
- Empty time slot = lost opportunity
- Fully booked day = maximally productive
- Day duration = first meeting prep to last meeting end

### Maker Schedule
- Only large chunks (4-6 hours)
- Only have ~2-3 blocks per day, ~10 blocks per week
- Empty calendar IS the opportunity
- One meeting in the morning kills 10% of weekly output
- Set start time but variable end time (work open to goal)
- A maker's "no" to a meeting is them keeping their commitment to meaningful work

### The Conflict
- Manager putting one meeting in = one of their 20+ daily slots
- Same meeting for maker = one of their 10 weekly blocks (10% of total output)
- Managers prevent the work they check in on
- When maker falls behind, manager's solution is to interrupt more (vicious cycle)

### Three-Pronged Solution

**For Managers:**
1. Understand the cost: you disrupt them TWICE (coordinating + the meeting itself)
2. Understand the maker's "no" -- it's not personal, it's productivity
3. Ask teams what ideal day looks like and follow it

**For Makers:**
1. Tell managers how you work (share this framework)
2. Have standard meeting times (e.g., Monday and Thursday afternoons)
3. Communicate when you'll be slow to respond
4. Actually work during maker time (don't prove suspicions right)

**For Organizations:**
1. Mandate quiet time (specific days or time blocks)
2. Wednesday quiet day at Acquisition.com (organization-wide)
3. Separate people into makers vs managers
4. Audit meetings: "Do I need to do this again? Is there a process/person instead?"

### Hormozi's Personal Calendar

- Manager day: "Just destroy me. Put everything in there."
- Maker days: completely empty except gym
- Leila: inverted (4 manager days, 1 maker day + weekends for maker work)

## System for Outworking Everyone

### Redefining Work
- Work = Outputs = Volume x Leverage
- Not force x distance, not time on clock, not effort expended
- "How much money you get out for the time you put in"

### The 4 Growth Levers
1. Get more customers (more traffic OR higher conversion)
2. Make customers worth more (raise prices OR decrease COGS OR get them to buy more times)

### Activities That Map to Levers
- More traffic: run ads, do outreach, make content, use affiliates (4 hours/day)
- Higher conversion: track close rate, watch game tape, practice scripts daily, pre-sale questionnaires
- "If you look at your schedule and you're not laddering up to these four things, you're not doing the stuff that makes money"

### Finding Time: Buying Back 96 Hours/Month
- Food/groceries/prep/cleaning: 50 hours/month -> $800 to replace
- House cleaning: 25 hours/month -> $500 to replace
- Laundry: 16 hours/month -> $200 to replace
- Total: 96 hours/month for $1,500/month
- "That's two full work weeks every single month"
- If you make more than $15/hour, this trade is obvious
- One-time investment -- can only pull this trigger once

### Free Time to Reclaim
- Following sports: 4 hours/week (cost to eliminate: $0)
- Watching TV: 21 hours/week ($0)
- Scrolling social media: 17 hours/week ($0)

### The 30x Multiplier
- Someone doing 96 productive hours/week vs someone doing 3 productive hours/week
- "That's how someone can move 30 times faster than you"
- Both people "work all day" -- the difference is what counts as real work

### Habits That Served Hormozi
- Wake up 4am, work until 9am (first 8 years of career)
- Decide the one project night before (never more than 3 things)
- Dark room, no windows, earplugs, no notifications
- "There are no emergencies. There's 911 and there's your business not growing for the third year."
- Same bedtime every night including weekends
- Set alarm for bedtime, not wake-up time
- Weekends = 104 days/year of uninterrupted time

## MAKER-MANAGER CALENDAR SYSTEM (Deep Pass Batch 2 -- Expanded)

### V1 (Starting Out): Maker Time in Off-Hours
- 5-9am before work, both weekend days
- = 9 maker blocks/week
- Best for solopreneurs still in a day job or running operations

### V2 (Intermediate): 50/50 Split
- First half of day = maker, second half = manager
- Schedule meetings back-to-front (avoids dead time between meetings)
- Ensures makers get uninterrupted morning blocks

### V3 (Advanced): Dedicated Days
- 4 maker days, 1 manager day (Alex's schedule)
- Or inverted: 4 manager days, 1 maker day (Leila's schedule)
- Organization-wide quiet day (Wednesday at acquisition.com)
- Manager day: "Just destroy me. Put everything in there."
- Maker days: completely empty except gym

### Pro Tips
- At end of every meeting, ask "Do I need to do this again?" and "Is there a person or system that can replace this?"
- Zeigarnik effect: open loops (pending meetings) eat brain power even before they happen
- A single meeting in a maker's morning destroys 10% of their weekly output (one of ~10 blocks)
- For a manager, same meeting is 1 of 100+ slots -- costs maker 10x
- When makers fall behind, manager's instinct is to check in MORE -> creates vicious cycle
