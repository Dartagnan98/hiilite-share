## No-Based Questions / The "Saying No" Close

Source: Chris Voss (Never Split the Difference) + Richard Schwartz (furrier)

### Psychology
- People feel safer saying "no" -- it preserves autonomy, they feel at ease
- Saying "yes" makes people feel vulnerable, responsible, committed
- Decision fatigue at end of day makes people even less likely to say "yes"

### How It Works
Instead of yes-based questions, phrase as no-based:
- YES version: "Are you excited to move forward today?" (hard to get yes)
- NO version: "Would you be opposed to moving forward today?" (easy to get "no, I'm not opposed")

### No-Based Phrases
- "Would you be opposed to..."
- "Would it be completely unreasonable for us to..."
- "Would it be completely out of left field for me to say..."
- "Would you be against..."
- "You don't want anything else, do you?"

### The Cheesecake Factory Example
- "Hey babe, you want to go to Cheesecake Factory?" -- "No, I don't want to go."
- "Would you be against going to Cheesecake Factory?" -- "Well, I'm not against it." -- "Great, let's go."

### The Richard Schwartz Fur Store Story (95% Upsell Close Rate)
Richard Schwartz, 4th generation furrier in Baltimore. Dominated the Maryland/Pennsylvania market.

Process: Customer comes in to store fur coat for summer. Son David says:
"What we're going to do is store and condition your coat. You don't want anything else, do you?"

Customer says: "No, I don't want anything else."

But "conditioning" was the upsell (brushing, shampooing -- extra couple hundred dollars). By saying "no, I don't want anything else," they agreed to the conditioning without realizing it was an upsell.

Then: Give free fur ear muffs. "Well, you need to store and condition these too." Customer puts them right back with the coat.

Then: Inspect coat. "Want to take care of this loose button? Want us to sew this seam? You don't want anything else, do you?" -- "No." They keep adding services.

**Result: 95% upsell take rate** vs typical 20-30%.

"People are so subconsciously wired to say no to upsells that they say yes by saying no."

### Three Types of Sales Questions
1. **Yes-based:** "Sound great?" "Is this the most amazing thing?" (hardest to get agreement)
2. **Neutral:** "Fair enough?" "Does that sound reasonable?" "Does that sound fair?" (easier)
3. **No-based:** "Would you be opposed to...?" (easiest to get agreement)

## The Assumed Close

### Origin Story
Alex was selling supplements at his gym. Went 0 for 20+ trying to pitch supplements with jargon (hypertrophy, lean body mass, etc).

Finally, with a well-dressed customer, instead of pitching benefits, he just asked:
"Do you want chocolate or vanilla for your protein?"
"Which one do you like?" "Chocolate." "I'll take one of those."
"For the pre-workout, do you want kiwi or strawberry?" "I'll do kiwi."
"Do you want to just use the card you have on file?" "Yeah, that's fine."

**Key insight:** "I didn't ask her WHETHER she wanted them. I asked her WHICH ONE she wanted."

### How It Works
Prospect chooses between two options that BOTH involve buying from you, rather than choosing whether to purchase at all.

### Results
- Normal close rate: 1 in 3, 1 in 4, or 1 in 2
- Assumed close as upsell: 9 out of 10, 9.5 out of 10

### Why It Works
- Selling to a customer who already knows and trusts you
- Purchase has already happened (card on file)
- Either/or option where both = buying

### Business Impact
- Supplement sales profit equaled or exceeded facility profit
- Upsells covered ALL advertising expense
- Service revenue became pure profit since acquisition costs were covered by upsells

### Application
Look at what customers normally buy alongside your service:
- Fitness: clothing, shoes, at-home equipment, apps, groceries, supplements
- Any service: identify the next natural purchase in their journey
- Weave those into your sales process as either/or decisions

## The Comparison Close (Handling "Competitor is Cheaper")

### The Stock Analogy
"Is Amazon stock more expensive than Bank of America stock? Yes. Which one do you want to own? Price is what you pay, value is what you get. If you're getting nothing for $200, it's still a bad investment."

### The Trainer Analogy
"I got a kid down the street who'll train you for $10/hour. Whose training do you think is better? Why do you think he charges $10? You get what you pay for."

### The Comparison Close Process
1. "Let's pretend price isn't an option. Let's say we're both priced the exact same amount. Which one would you rather do?"
2. They say you. Ask: "Why?"
3. They start listing reasons (coaching, higher ticket, makes more sense)
4. Keep going: "Anything else? What about ROI? Risk? Track record?"
5. They spend 3 minutes selling you on why you're better
6. "Right. You can understand why we're not $200."

### The Time Frame Close
"How important is your time? Do you want to waste 3 more months not hitting your goal trying another Mickey Mouse thing?"

### The Logic Close
"Do you think if he was really the best in the industry, the most likely to help you hit your goal, he would charge the same as a boot camp client? Of course not."

### The Buy Nice or Buy Twice Close
"You can buy nice or buy twice. It's up to you."

## The Downsell Framework

### Core Belief
"Everyone buys something. Everyone has some money. Everyone has this problem. We should find a way to help everyone."

### The Key: Exchange (Reciprocity)
Each step down requires the prospect to do something in return. This maintains your frame and ethical positioning.

Cards to play (things you can ask them to do in exchange for lower price):
1. Longer commitment term
2. Leave a testimonial
3. Leave reviews
4. Bring a friend
5. Add bonuses to original package (prefer asking them to do MORE rather than removing bonuses)

### The Step-Down Sequence (Fitness Example at $299)

**Offer 1 (Full price):** "$299 for 3 weeks. Fair enough?" -- No.

**Step-Down 1 (Half price + commitment):** "What if we did $49/week for 12 weeks with a commitment? Fair enough?" -- No.

**Step-Down 2 (Remove commitment):** "Don't worry about the commitment. Just get started today for $49. Pay as you go. Fair enough?" -- No.

**Step-Down 3 (Lower + testimonial):** "How about $39/week if you leave me a testimonial at the end? Fair enough?" -- No.

**Step-Down 4 (Lowest + referral):** "$29/week. Pay as you go. Just bring a friend in the first 30 days so I can at least cover my costs. Fair enough?" -- No.

If they say no after 5-6 step-downs: "It's not a money thing. You lost them earlier in the sale."

### Tonality Rules for Downselling
- Always use "Fair enough?" as the CTA (lowers the bar to agreement vs "Sound good?" or "Sound great?")
- Ask it like you actually want them to say yes
- Be collaborative: "Let's figure out a way to make this work"
- Acknowledge increasing ridiculousness in a light tone -- it pulls on internal reciprocity mechanism

### Why It Works
- Reciprocity: people struggle to keep saying no when you keep accommodating
- After they refused 4+ accommodations, internal psychological pressure builds
- You're on the same side of the table: "Let's figure this out together"

### Post-Close Goal
"You're just trying to get your foot in the door. In 7-14 days they'll finally trust you and buy the full-price program. Don't worry about it."

## Ghost Products / Sacrificial Lambs Technique

### Origin Story
Alex ran out of a supplement during a launch. Instead of skirting around it, told customers: "You can get this one cheaper down the street at Costco. You don't need to get this one from me."

After crossing that one off, he said "But you do want this and this from us" -- and EVERYONE bought.

### The Framework
1. Carry 1-2 products on your recommendation list that you intentionally DON'T sell
2. Proactively recommend the prospect get those items elsewhere (cheaper, just as good)
3. This builds massive trust -- you acted in their self-interest
4. Then prescribe the remaining products -- close rate skyrockets

**Result: 100% close rate** for Alex personally. 80-90% for people he trained who'd never sold before (retail environment).

### Rules
- Ghost products should be your LOWEST margin items
- Give away the low-margin stuff to gain trust, keep the high-margin stuff
- You can eventually stop even carrying those products -- they exist purely as trust-building tools

### The Prescriptive Close (Pairing Ghost Products with Assumed Close)
After recommending what to buy and what to skip:
1. Tell them HOW to take/use each product (associate with existing habit)
   - "What do you do every morning no matter what? Brush your teeth? Put this next to your toothbrush."
   - "Put a piece of tape on it with a '2' -- take two every morning"
2. Go through each product with usage instructions
3. Close: "Did you just want to use the card you have on file?"

No new wallet action. No new purchase decision. One-click upsell.

### If They Push Back on Price
- "Are you on a budget?"
- "Would you like me to order these in order of importance?"
- Remove the lowest-impact item: "This would be the lowest one I'd remove. Does this work?"
- If still no: "Really getting into bare bones here. I could take this out, but you're gonna have to eat a pound of broccoli" -- reintroduce the problem the product solves
- Find money from old habits: "Are you going to stop going out? Drink less? How much do you spend on that? We just found some money."

### The Table Metaphor
"The goal as a salesperson is to move to their side of the table. Start the conversation across the table. End shoulder to shoulder, both looking at the decision together with the same information."

## The Damaging Admission (Persuasion Technique)

Source: Eminem in 8 Mile + Viagra commercial

### Core Principle
The word "BUT" acts as an amplifier for whatever comes AFTER it, and a diminisher for whatever comes BEFORE it.

### How to Use It
**Wrong order (positive BUT negative):** "You're going to make a ton of money BUT it's going to be hundreds of hours of work." -- Attention goes to the negatives.

**Right order (negative BUT positive):** "You're going to have to go through hundreds of hours of videos, take 5 hours a day to execute BUT you're going to make more money than ever in your life." -- Attention goes to the positive.

### The More Negatives, The More Believable
"The more negatives I can say in the beginning, the more believable the thing I say right afterwards is."

### The Viagra Example
Warning label: "If an erection lasts longer than 4 hours, contact a medical professional."

The "risk" amplified the benefit. Every guy heard that and thought: "I could have an erection for 4 hours? Sign me up."

Equivalent: "WARNING: When you sign up with GymLaunch, you're going to massively increase your tax burden. We are not liable for the amount of taxes you'll have to pay as a result of the money we're going to make."

### The Reviews Example
"Gym Launch has tons of one-star reviews. BUT we also have thousands of five-star reviews. Would you rather eat at a restaurant with 4.7 stars and 3,000 ratings, or 5 stars with 19?"

The negative reviews give credence to the positive ones.

### Application
- Weave into copy, especially near calls to action
- Use in sales presentations
- Be truthful in the negatives -- the more genuinely damaging they are, the more believable the positive
- Own all deficiencies with a single statement to increase trust

## The Seven Universal Closes

Rather than memorizing dozens of specific objection overcomes, memorize 7 universal closes that work for any objection:

### Close 1: "That's the Reason" Close
Whatever reason they give for not buying, flip it:
- "I don't have time." -> "The fact that you don't have time is probably the reason you need this more than anyone else."
- "I don't have money." -> "The fact that you don't have money is exactly why you need to do this -- because this is exactly what our product helps you solve."

For consumer products: "It means you're going to care more than anyone else because you're so invested."
For B2B: "The problem you're citing as the reason not to buy is the problem we solve."

### Key Insight
"All objection overcomes exist literally just to give you an excuse to ask again."

Someone says an objection -> you respond -> "If we resolved it this way, would that work?" -> They say yes or give another objection -> repeat.

"You're only going to pick up 20, maybe 30% of your sales after the no. The vast majority of selling happens prior to the ask."

### Handling Price Objections
- "Let's pretend price isn't an option. If we're both priced the same, which would you rather do?"
- They say you. Ask "why?" Let them spend 3 minutes selling themselves
- Then: "For that reason, you can understand why we're not $200"
- Stock analogy: "Amazon is more expensive than Bank of America. Price is what you pay, value is what you get."
- "Do you think if he was really the best in the industry he would charge the same as a bootcamp client?"
- "Buy nice or buy twice"

### The "No to Say Yes" Furrier Details
- Richard Schwarz furriers technique expanded: "You don't want anything else, do you?"
- People pre-programmed to say "no" to upsells accidentally agree to assumed close
- Result: 90%+ uptake on upsells vs industry 20-30%
- Conditioning was the upsell, presented as included, then asked "you don't want anything else?"
- Customer says no, confirming the bundled upsell
- Then: inspect coat -> "want us to fix this loose button?" -> "you don't want anything else, do you?" -> keeps stacking

### Assumed Close Upsell System
1. Complete primary sale first
2. Present either/or options that both involve buying: "Chocolate or vanilla?"
3. Use card already on file: "Want to use the card you have?"
4. Never ask IF they want it; ask WHICH one they want
5. Close rate: 9-9.5 out of 10 vs standard 1 in 3-4
6. Can offset entire cost of customer acquisition through upsells alone

### The Price + Barter Close
- Can't afford full price? Lower price IF they provide: reviews, testimonials, daily posts, referrals
- Trade $100 for advertising value worth more than $100
- "Do you want to do what Susan did? She cleans the gym, introduced 3 friends, posts daily"

### Feature Downsell Gets MORE People to Buy the Expensive Thing
- Remove something valuable + tiny price decrease -> people re-upsell themselves on original
- Friend went from 25% close rate to 75% by adding feature downsell
- "People see the value of what you removed after they see the price difference"

### The Trampoline Close
- 20+ in-person consults/day. Bought mini trampoline, bounced during meetings
- "You cannot be in a bad mood on a trampoline"
- Before that: did a cartwheel before every handshake
- "What they ultimately wanted to buy was the feeling I was giving them"

## BANT QUALIFICATION SCRIPTS (Batch 7)

### Full BANT Set Call Script
- **Budget:** "This is a four-figure+ investment. Just want to make sure this isn't a shock."

## SIX PERSUASION TOOLS APPLIED (Deep Pass -- Batch 4)

### Reciprocity
- Waiter + 1 mint = 3% more tips. 2 mints = 14%. Put 1 down, walk away, come back with another = **23% more tips**
- Retail: cold water at door = ~$5/day, highest ROI in business
- Alex gave free gum in middle school: "People were way nicer to me"

### Consistency
- "Are you the type of person who invests in themselves?" (Yes) -> later: "Since you're that type of person..."
- Small sign -> massive sign study: compliance skyrocketed after initial small commitment

### Social Proof
- Floor-to-ceiling framed 5-star reviews in gym lobby + before/after photos
- "How much proof would you need? At what point is your requirement for proof more unreasonable than the proof I have?"
- Laugh tracks make people laugh more -- we're conditioned to follow others

### Liking (ACA Framework)
- Acknowledge what they said, Compliment what they did, Ask another question
- No cap on flattery if genuine. Even paid compliments increase liking
- Trampoline + cartwheels before meetings for energy/liking transfer

### Authority
- Printed certifications for self and every trainer (including internal ones)
- "Which certifications do you want?" -- nobody ever knew
- Track record > charisma

### Scarcity/Urgency
- De Beers keeps diamonds in vaults for artificial scarcity
- Luxury stores kept COVID capacity limits after restrictions lifted
- Amazon shows limited stock. "If you actually put true scarcity, you will sell more"
- Loss language > gain language: "Don't miss out" > "Don't you want to get..."

## CLOSING ADDITIONS (Batch 5-6)

### The "No Sale" Playbook
- Customer declines everything. Salvage: free at-home program + comeback nutrition consult
- At consult: close on supplements. Same close rate, higher ticket ($300 vs $200)
- Key: "We tried to solve their problem in a way they didn't want. They wanted a magic pill."

### Classic Upsell Structure
- "You can't have X without Y" -- fundamental framework
- Must feel as required as possible without being required (stays ethical)
- Seamless process = 80%+ take rate
- If getting 30% on upsells, "you're not making it assumed enough"
- McDonald's fries+coke: ~80% of people getting them. That's the benchmark.

### Underdog Sales Positioning Script
- "You don't want to work with [big company] because to them, you're just a number"
- "You're never going to meet [founder]. You'll get assigned some account rep who doesn't care"
- "Who you're really comparing is not my company versus [leader]. It's me versus their rep."
